The Lafayette Problem — Why This Matters More Than Most Sites
Lafayette Avg RH: 75-80%
ASHRAE target for compute: 40-60% RH. NVL72 racks have internal fans on PSUs, NVLink switches, networking — they pull ambient air. At 80% RH, you're blowing warm wet air over hot electronics 24/7. Corrosion, condensation, shortened life.
NVL72 Is NOT Fully Liquid Cooled
GPU chips = liquid cooled via coldplates. PSUs, NVSwitches, networking cards = air cooled with internal fans. The ambient air quality inside the building matters. This is the hidden problem most northern operators never face.
Solar Solves It During The Day
Sunny days = highest humidity load AND highest solar output. Run dehumidifiers direct off solar during peak sun hours. When it's hottest and most humid, we have the most free power to fight it. The problem and the solution peak at the same time.

Capex Reality
Yes, This Costs More Upfront
Dehumidification adds to Capex vs a dry-climate site. But the alternative is shortened component life, higher failure rates, and warranty issues. This is a required line item for Louisiana — not optional.
Solar Offsets The Opex
Desiccant dehumidifiers run on electricity. 2 MW of solar powers them for free during peak humidity hours. The ongoing electricity cost is near-zero during the day. Capex hit, minimal Opex.
Waste Heat = Free Regeneration
Munters desiccant wheels need heat to regenerate. NVL72 racks produce massive waste heat. Route exhaust air through desiccant regeneration loop — the racks pay for their own dehumidification.

Loading dock — door width, door height, dock height off grade, and whether a standard flatbed can back straight in.

6
Floor condition — walk every square foot. Mark cracks, low spots, drains, existing penetrations. Tap suspect areas.
NVL72 rack = 3,000 lbs on 4 feet. Concrete must be verified before you commit rack positions. Any soft spots = PE inspection before install.
7
Concrete floor thickness — find any slab edge or crack where you can see depth. Probe with a screwdriver at any spall.
4 inch slab = marginal for 3,000 lb point loads. 6 inch+ = fine. Determines if you need pedestals or reinforcement.
8
Truss condition — photograph each truss end where it bears on the brick wall. Look for rot, insect damage, broken members, wall separation.
The bearing point is the failure point. Everything else can be sistered. A failed bearing = wall repair + new bearing seat.
9
Roof — from inside, scan for daylight through wood slat decking. Any staining, soft spots, or active drips.
A compute clean room cannot have roof leaks. Seal the roof before a single rack goes in. Know the full scope now.
10
Brick wall condition — all four walls. Any bowing, leaning, cracks, mortar loss, efflorescence (white salt staining = moisture intrusion).
Brick is your insulation and your humidity barrier. Any breach = moisture path into the compute space.

Utilities — Mark Everything On A Sketch
GAS
Where does the trunk line enter the Middle High building? Follow it, photograph every fitting, valve, and tee. Find the main shutoff. Measure pipe diameter.
Pipe diameter tells you what gas flow you're starting with. A 2-inch line won't feed a 1 MW genset — you'll need a new service drop. Know this before calling ATMOS.
ELEC
Any existing service panels, disconnects, meters still in the buildings. Note location, voltage markings, amperage if labeled. Note direction the power poles run toward the site from Pin Hook.
LUS Pin Hook substation = 13.8 kV service voltage (confirmed in our files). Knowing where the poles run tells you the shortest path for your new service drop.
WATER
Any existing water lines, standpipes, drains still intact. Fire suppression piping — trace it, find the main control valve, photograph the riser and any sprinkler heads.
Existing fire suppression infrastructure = huge value. If the pipes are intact and the riser is good, you may be able to reuse most of it with new heads and a new suppression agent. A PE can evaluate.
FIBER
Any existing conduit entering the buildings — even if empty. Note entry point location and direction it runs toward the street.
LFT Fiber (municipal 25G) serves the area. An existing conduit stub = pull fiber same day. No conduit = short dig, but you need to know the entry point before you design it.
Water Tower
1
Height — ground to bottom of tank, and ground to top overflow. Measure or laser.
Gravity head pressure from the tank drives the cooling return loop. More height = more pressure = less pump work. This number goes into the hydraulic design.
2
Inlet and outlet pipe sizes at the base. Any valves present and condition.
Sets the pipe sizing for the cooling loop connection. Also tells you if existing plumbing can be reused.
3
Tank shell condition — any rust-through, holes, visible patch repairs. Is the ladder climbable safely?
A structural inspection will be required before any hydraulic use. This is your pre-inspection walkaround — know what you're dealing with before spending money on the formal inspection.
4
Distance from water tower base to Middle High building.
Cooling loop pipe run length. Longer run = more friction loss = affects pump sizing.
Concrete Pad — Infrastructure Yard
1
Full dimensions of the pad. Any sections that are cracked, heaved, or deteriorated.

Sets the 800V DC cable run length from MVSST on the pad to the building. Longer runs = larger conductor = more copper cost. Under 200 ft is clean.
