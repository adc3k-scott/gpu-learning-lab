 Executive Summary 
 The  rapid  escalation  of  AI-driven  workloads  is  pushing  data  center  power  systems  beyond  the 
 limits  of  traditional  architectures.  Rack  power  densities  are  increasing  sharply,  load  profiles  are 
 becoming  more  dynamic,  and  efficiency,  scalability,  and  grid  interaction  are  now  first-order 
 design  constraints.  To  address  these  challenges,  the  data  center  industry  is  increasingly 
 exploring  direct  current  (DC)  power  distribution  as  a  complementary  approach  to  conventional 
 AC-based systems. 
 This  white  paper,  developed  within  The  Open  Compute  Project,  a  collaborative  industry  initiative 
 focused  on  open,  scalable,  and  efficient  data  center  infrastructure,  provides  a  high-level 
 overview  of  DC  power  distribution  architectures  for  data  center  facilities.  It  examines  key  design 
 concepts,  system  boundaries,  and  tradeoffs  across  power  conversion,  grounding  and  earthing, 
 protection, energy storage integration, and regulatory considerations. 
 Rather  than  promoting  a  single  solution,  the  paper  presents  representative  DC  power 
 distribution  approaches  observed  or  under  evaluation  across  the  industry.  It  is  intended  to 
 support  a  shared  understanding  among  data  center  operators,  designers,  equipment 
 manufacturers,  utilities,  and  Authorities  Having  Jurisdiction  (AHJs),  enabling  informed 
 discussion  and  consistent  interpretation  as  DC  architectures  continue  to  evolve.  This  white 
 paper represents an initial framework and identifies areas requiring further development.

 1.  Introduction 
 As  data  center  power  systems  evolve  to  support  increasingly  demanding  compute 
 environments,  there  is  growing  interest  in  alternative  power  distribution  approaches  that  can 
 accommodate  higher  power  densities,  dynamic  load  behavior,  and  expanding  system  scale. 
 Among  these  approaches,  direct  current  (DC)  power  distribution  has  emerged  as  an  area  of 
 active evaluation and early deployment across the industry. 
 DC  power  architectures  introduce  different  design  considerations  than  traditional  AC  systems, 
 particularly  in  areas  such  as  power  conversion  boundaries,  grounding  and  earthing,  protection 
 and  fault  behavior,  integration  of  energy  storage,  and  interaction  with  upstream  utility 
 infrastructure.  These  considerations  span  multiple  layers  of  the  data  center,  from  facility-level 
 power  distribution  to  zone-  and  rack-level  implementations,  and  involve  close  coordination 
 between equipment design, system architecture, and regulatory requirements. 
 This  paper  focuses  on  describing  these  considerations  at  a  conceptual  and  architectural  level.  It 
 outlines  commonly  discussed  DC  power  distribution  configurations,  identifies  key  system 
 components  and  interfaces,  and  highlights  technical  tradeoffs  that  may  influence  design 
 decisions.  The  intent  is  to  establish  a  common  technical  framework  rather  than  to  recommend 
 or  mandate  a  specific  topology.  However,  this  white  paper  represents  an  initial  framework.  Areas 
 requiring further development are identified within the document. 
 The  content  is  written  for  a  broad  audience,  including  data  center  operators,  designers, 
 equipment  manufacturers,  utilities,  and  Authorities  Having  Jurisdiction  (AHJs).  All  architectures 
 discussed  remain  subject  to  applicable  codes,  standards,  equipment  certification,  and  AHJ 
 approval.  As  data  center  power  systems  continue  to  evolve,  multiple  AC  and  DC  distribution 
 models  are  expected  to  coexist,  shaped  by  site-specific  requirements,  regional  practices,  and 
 ecosystem maturity.

 2.  Compliance with Open Compute Project Tenets 
 This  white  paper  aligns  with  the  core  tenets  of  Open  Compute  Project  by  promoting  openness, 
 efficiency,  impact,  scalability,  and  sustainability  in  the  context  of  data  center  DC  power 
 distribution.  As  a  conceptual  and  architectural  document  rather  than  a  hardware  or  software 
 contribution,  its  primary  objective  is  to  enable  shared  understanding,  informed  discussion,  and 
 ecosystem alignment across a diverse set of stakeholders. 
 2.1.  Openness 
 This  paper  supports  openness  by  presenting  DC  power  distribution  concepts,  architectures,  and 
 design  considerations  in  a  vendor-neutral  and  non-proprietary  manner.  It  avoids  prescribing 
 specific  products  or  implementations  and  instead  focuses  on  commonly  discussed  approaches, 
 tradeoffs,  and  system-level  implications.  By  establishing  a  shared  technical  vocabulary  and 
 framing  considerations  applicable  across  regions  and  deployment  models,  the  document 
 enables broad participation, review, and extension by the wider data center ecosystem. 
 2.2.  Efﬁciency 
 Efficiency  is  addressed  through  a  system-level  examination  of  DC  power  distribution,  including 
 the  potential  reduction  of  power  conversion  stages,  improved  utilization  of  conductors,  and 
 closer  integration  of  energy  storage.  The  paper  emphasizes  architectural  choices  that  can 
 influence  electrical  efficiency,  power  density,  and  operational  effectiveness,  while  recognizing 
 that realized gains depend on site-specific design, load profiles, and implementation details. 
 2.3.  Impact 
 The  document  aims  to  create  industry  impact  by  clarifying  emerging  DC  power  distribution 
 concepts  that  are  increasingly  relevant  to  next-generation,  high-density  data  centers.  By 
 providing  a  high-level,  accessible  reference  for  designers,  operators,  equipment  manufacturers,

 3.  Background Context 
 3.1.  Data Center Transition to Direct Current: Background and Needs 
 As  AI  models  scale  in  complexity,  significant  architectural  changes  are  envisioned  to  the  IT  rack 
 to  support  higher  compute  density  and  speed  up  the  overall  compute  cluster  by  minimizing 
 network  packet  loss  (through  staying  within  the  copper  layer  boundary  and  reducing  reliance  on 
 optical  interconnections).  Low  Voltage  Direct  Current  (LVDC)  Power  Distribution  (≤1500  VDC) 
 will  soon  function  as  a  critical  link  between  the  IT  rack  AI  factory  compute  load  and  incoming 
 data  center  facility  power  –  whether  from  the  utility  service,  local  production,  or  both.  To  address 
 this  critical  and  rapidly  developing  area,  The  Open  Compute  Project  (OCP)  has  organized  a 
 Power Distribution  sub-project within the Data Center  Facility project. 
 Initial  adoption  of  the  Mt.  Diablo  Power  Rack  (side  car)  enables  the  ORv3  HPR  IT  Rack  to 
 increase  from  AC-supplied  HPR  v1  33kW  or  HPR  v2  72kW  power  shelves  to  DC-supplied  HPR 
 v4  100kW  power  shelves  within  the  same  1  OU  space  footprint  –  itself  a  39%  increase  in  power 
 density  per  power  shelf,  made  possible  in  part  by  removal  of  the  AC-DC  conversion  stage. 
 Additionally,  moving  in-rack  energy  storage  (in-rack  2  OU  BBU  and  optional  CBUs)  from  the  IT 
 Rack  to  the  Power  Rack  has  the  further  advantage  of  freeing  up  approximately  12-16  OU  space 
 footprint  within  systems  that  use  in-rack  storage,  yielding  a  further  27-36%  increase  in  available 
 44 OU IT Rack space for further compute power. 
 Ultimately,  achieving  the  roofline  power  potential  of  HPR  v4  requires  addressing  the  further 
 bottleneck  of  existing  50  VDC  vertical  bus  distribution  within  the  IT  rack  (as  even  ORW  racks 
 with  horizontal  bus  face  power  limitations  beyond  the  100’s  of  kW).  For  this  reason,  higher 
 voltage  (e.g.  800  VDC)  vertical  bus  represents  the  next  progression  for  the  IT  rack,  enabling  it  to 
 realize the potential of its 1+ MW roof line (see  Figure 1  ).

 3.2.  Document Scope 
 This  white  paper  is  intended  to  address  LVDC  power  distribution  and  its  role  as  an  interface 
 between  incoming  Medium  Voltage  Alternating  Current  (MVAC)  power  (presently  in  the  gray 
 space)  with  IT  rack  compute  in  the  white  space.  Section  4  and  Section  8  of  this  white  paper  are 
 intended  to  focus  on  the  conversion  of  MVAC  to  LVDC,  whether  by  Solid  State  Transformer 
 (SST)  or  other  conversion  components.  Future  editions  of  this  white  paper  may  also  consider 
 other  possible  LVDC  sources,  their  interconnection,  and  varying  levels  of  available  fault  current. 
 While  energy  storage  is  also  included,  direct  connection  is  excluded  (i.e.  a  converter  interface  is 
 required). 
 At  present,  the  scope  is  limited  to  1500  VDC,  thereby  excluding  what  is  commonly  referred  to  as 
 Medium  Voltage  Direct  Current  (MVDC)  which  finds  usage  in  other  applications  such  as  2000 
 VDC  for  solar  PV  renewable  energy  sources.  Future  editions  of  this  white  paper  may  also 
 consider  utilization  of  an  MVDC  underlay  grid  and  SST  conversion  (or  otherwise)  from  MVDC  to 
 LVDC. 
 Additionally,  while  Direct  Current  usage  for  industrial  /  commercial  building  microgrids  is  typically 
 focused  on  loosely  regulated  droop  control  and  interoperable  component  operation  across  a 
 wide  band  of  continuous  voltage  (reducing  round  trip  losses  for  energy  storage  through  the 
 usable  state-of-charge  range),  Direct  Current  usage  in  data  centers  is  focused  on  tightly 
 regulated  operation  in  a  narrow  band  of  continuous  voltage  optimized  for  maximum  power 
 delivery at the limit of component voltage class capability. 
 These  differences  in  application  lead  to  a  further  consideration  regarding  the  future  trajectory  of 
 Direct  Current  usage  in  the  data  center  facility  for  cooling,  lighting,  and  other  non-regenerative 
 loads  which  have  the  potential  to  take  advantage  of  efficiency  from  higher  DC  voltage 
 distribution  and  reduced  AC  conversion  losses.  It  is  likely  that  such  usage  will  reflect  the  higher 
 voltage  limits  of  IT  rack  compute  loads  rather  than  the  traditional  limits  of  DC  microgrids.

 4.  System Architecture Methodology 
 4.1.  Grid Connection 
 This  subsection  describes,  at  a  high  level,  the  connection  between  loads  and  power  distribution. 
 In  order  to  simplify  the  discussion  we  consider  two  topologies:  a  single  “line-up”  for  an  MV  to  LV 
 power  block  to  supply  the  AI  load  (e.g.  3  MW+),  and  a  Direct  Current  power  generation  source 
 feeding  a  similar  AI  load  /  Data  Center  room.  Future  papers  will  address  full  distribution  systems 
 as the focus of this paper is to be a foundational document to be built upon with further work. 
 4.1.1.  From MV to LV distribution 
 MV  gets  distributed  from  substation  into  smaller  power  nodes  that  feed  into  the  data  hall’s  and 
 mechanical  hall’s  LV  system.  These  power  nodes  consist  typically  in  a  MV  switchgear  setup  that 
 allows  a  redundant  routing  of  the  power,  e.g.  a  loop  feed,  that  ties  in  several  nodes  (connection 
 points)  together  and  is  fed  from  two  different  sources.  Each  node  will  convert  the  power  needed 
 into  LV  power  being  used  at  a  load  nearby  via  a  MV/LV  transformer.  These  nodes  are  not  placed 
 in  parallel,  but  can  be  interlaced  on  the  LV  side  with  a  reserve  bus  topology  (for  N+1  to  make  N) 
 or  dynamic  bus  topology  (2N)  to  provide  redundancy.  One  node  can  have  several  feeds,  with 
 each feed having a dedicated breaker and protection setup. 
 The  current  standard  topology  consists  in  only  AC  type  distribution,  with  eventual  conversion 
 from  AC  to  DC  in  the  rack  to  DC  infrastructure  located  downstream  from  the  LV  AC  switchgear, 
 for  each  dedicated  feed.  Building  on  this  topology,  a  first  step  into  conversion  into  a  DC  network 
 could  be  the  addition  of  a  DC  network  at  the  LV  root  of  the  power  node  (e.g.  as  described  by  the 
 Mt.  Diablo  specification).  One  transformer  would  be  feeding  at  the  same  time  AC  and  DC 
 networks.  Transforming  a  data  center’s  power  supply  is  more  than  a  technical  upgrade–it  is  an 
 adoption  in  a  staged  approach  and  a  strategic  step  toward  securing  performance,  efficiency,  and 
 reliability.  The  first  principle  driver  is  to  support  compute  densification  and  congestion 
 management.

 4.2.  Redundancy Levels 
 Currently,  the  targeted  reliability  level  is  99.9%  for  training  AI  data  centers  and  99.999% 
 for inference AI data centers. 
 Generally,  power  electronics  are  the  components  that  have  the  highest  failure  rate  in  a 
 data  center  (e.g.,  power  shelves,  UPS)  and  this  has  been  managed  by  a  few  strategies 
 such as N+1 power blocks, and maintenance bypass. 
 However,  DC  distribution  does  not  allow  for  a  maintenance  bypass  strategy  of  an 
 uninterruptible  power  supply,  so  there  needs  to  be  redundancy  at  the  power  block  level 
 and potentially at the power distribution level as well. 
 If not, there needs to be some kind of upstream DC Static Transfer Switch. 
 Even  for  99.9%  availability  level,  there  needs  to  be  some  kind  of  catcher  system(type  of 
 uninterruptible  power  supply)  to  prevent  single  point  of  failure  at  the  AC/DC  conversion 
 block. 
 To  achieve  99.999%,  if  the  AC/DC  conversion  block  has  a  high  failure  rate,  then  there 
 needs to be redundancy at the AC/DC conversion block level. 
 If  there’s  modularity  within  the  AC/DC  conversion  block,  redundancy  at  that  level  may 
 reduce the redundancy requirement at the AC/DC conversion block level 
 Other points of consideration: 
 ●  Failure rate scales with ambient temperature 
 ●  Useful  life  of  the  data  center  is  ~10  years  -  so  need  to  understand  how  the 
 failure  rate  changes  over  that  period  of  time;  including  on-site  spare 
 equipment for repairability 
 ●  For  applicable  equipment,  need  bypass  test  to  detect  fault  that  caused  the 
 failure of the power module

 5.  Voltage Levels and Power Quality 
 This  section  reviews  common  definitions,  introduces  key  concepts,  and  discusses  critical 
 considerations around voltage. 
 5.1.  Voltage Classes 
 While  many  sub  classifications  of  voltage  exist,  there  are  two  primary  classifications  commonly 
 recognized by codes and standards – low voltage and high voltage. 
 5.2.  Low Voltage (LV), Including Sub-Classes (e.g. ELV, LES) 
 5.2.1.  General Definition

 and  regulations  along  with  installation  codes  as  operation  at  or  below  1000  VAC  or  1500  VDC.

 AC peak as √2 factor above AC RMS voltage.)

 5.2.2.  Extra-Low Voltage (ELV)

 expected  (≤6  VAC  or  ≤15  VDC  otherwise)  and  supplying  voltage  from  a  battery,  safety  isolating 
 transformer,  or  safety  equivalent.  The  dependence  of  the  exact  voltage  threshold  for  ELV  on  the 
 conditions  aligns  with  the  more  general  definition  below.  Additional  criteria  specific  to  SELV

 5.6.  Voltage Bands in Active DC Microgrids 
 Passive  DC  microgrids  include  simple  loads  which  operate  independently,  based  on  the 
 presence  or  absence  of  source  power  within  a  defined  tolerance.  Active  DC  microgrids  are 
 capable  of  coordinating  multiple  sources,  loads,  and  prosumers,  and  typically  employ  voltage 
 droop  control  of  current  /  power.  Droop  controlled  devices  are  designed  to  operate  within 
 voltage  bands  and  may  also  be  combined  with  simple  loads,  provided  the  impact  to  system 
 behavior is limited (as further described in  IEC TR  63282  and  NPR 9090)  . 
 Active  DC  microgrid  voltage  bands  typically  provide  greater  nominal  voltage  flexibility  than  is 
 possible  in  AC,  where  frequency,  voltage,  and  phase  must  remain  synchronized  with  the  utility 
 for  rotating  production  sources  (acc.  to  UL  6200  )  and  inverters  (acc.  to  UL  1741  ).  Voltage 
 change  over  time  mirrors  the  power  balance  in  the  active  DC  microgrid,  i.e.,  if  more  power  is 
 needed  than  available,  voltage  declines  and  vice-versa.  This  property  can  be  used  for  power 
 management,  using  so-called  “droop  curves”  whereby  active  components  (e.g.,  converters) 
 adjust their power output according to the voltage measured at their terminals. 
 In  addition  to  the  voltage  band  descriptions  found  in  IEC  TR  63282  ,  droop  curve  descriptions 
 and  examples  are  also  available  in  the  system  description  documents  of  ODCA  and  Current/OS 
 based on LVDC commercial and industrial installations.

 5.6.3.  Narrow vs. Wide Voltage Bands 
 Traditionally,  DC  microgrid  design  has  embraced  wide  voltage  bands  with  ample  overvoltage 
 margin  in  band  B5  as  a  means  of  facilitating  “pre-designed”  DC  systems  constructed  from 
 interoperable  “off-the-shelf”  components  to  provide  interoperability  among  manufacturers, 
 optimize equipment usable life, and minimize custom engineering. 
 A  wide  operation  range  of  voltage  bands  B3  to  B4  for  normal  devices  and  including  B2  for 
 critical  loads,  has  the  additional  application  benefit  of  minimizing  round-trip  energy  storage 
 losses  by  aligning  with  a  typical  state-of-charge  range.  Additionally,  provisioning  voltage  band 
 B5  accommodates  voltage  swell  associated  with  unloading  of  simple  loads  and  regenerative 
 energy recuperation from motor drives.

 5.7.1.  Low Voltage Limit Considerations 
 As  can  be  seen  in  the  definitions  provided  in  Section  5.2  ,  the  limit  for  low  voltage  is  1500  VDC.

 average  voltage  whereas  the  other  definitions  provided  define  in  the  context  of  nominal  voltage 
 which does not have to be the same as the average voltage.

 A  further  consideration  is  that  while  ANSI  C84.1  does  not  define  an  analogous  nominal  voltage 
 of  1000  VAC,  the  5.08%  tolerance  it  commonly  applies  to  other  nominal  voltages  such  as  600V 
 results  in  a  value  of  1058  VAC  which  aligns  to  the  maximum  voltage  defined  in  ANSI  C37.50  for 
 power breakers. 
 Considering  further  on  this,  it  is  not  uncommon  to  utilize  equipment  rated  to  1000  VAC  in  a  lower 
 voltage,  e.g.  600  VAC  system.  The  issue  of  name  plate  labels  should  then  be  differentiated  for 
 systems  /  sources  as  compared  with  products  /  loads.  An  interesting  consideration  in  regard  to 
 sources  is  that  whereas  Distributed  Energy  Resources  are  required  to  accommodate  continuous 
 110%  overvoltage,  they  are  typically  utility  grid  following  and  thus  synchronize  with  the  supply  of 
 the  utility  rather  than  defining  their  own  voltage  level.  However,  notably  this  same  110% 
 continuous  overvoltage  level  is  carried  forward  to  grid  forming  operation  when  islanded  for  DER 
 systems according to UL 3001. 
 ANSI  C84.1  incorporates  a  factor  of  5%  for  line  losses  and  utilization  voltage.  Considering  that 
 the  same  line  losses  exist  between  a  DER  source  point  of  connection  (inverter  terminals)  and 
 the  point  of  common  coupling  with  utility  (at  the  meter),  it  is  reasonable  to  consider  that  a  source 
 tolerance  of  at  least  5%  is  generally  possible  –  so  the  question  comes  down  to  what  to  do  at  the 
 limit. 
 Ultimately, three likely sufficient but not necessary rules are as follows: 
 1)  Avoid  setpoints  above  1500  VDC.  Note  that  this  has  an  important  bearing  on  series 
 usage  of  800V  sources.  Due  to  the  possibility  of  1600V  operation,  max  voltage  limit 
 setpoint  restriction  will  be  necessary,  e.g.  at  750V  or  1500V.  There  is  analogous  max 
 current  limit  setpoint  restriction  defined  in  context  of  conductor  /  busbar  overload  control 
 via  Power  Control  Systems  compliant  with  UL  3141  Section  6.16  and  NEC  Article  130 
 Part II. 
 2)  Avoid  average  above  1500  VDC  (as  per  IEC  TS  63527).  This  may  come  with  a  caveat  to 
 be clarified regarding open terminal floating voltage as opposed to loaded condition. 
 3)  Avoid  extended  excursions  above  1500  VDC.  Additional  work  is  needed  to  define  a 
 proposal here on the basis of transient and swell definitions.

 6.  Polarities & System Earthing 
 This  chapter  defines  and  evaluates  polarity  configurations  and  earthing  schemes  used  in  direct 
 current  (DC)  data  center  power  systems.  Its  purpose  is  to  clarify  possible  design  options, 
 identify  technical  tradeoffs,  and  support  consistent  implementation  and  commonalities  across 
 different system architectures. 
 The  chapter  reviews  unipolar  and  bipolar  systems,  including  variants  with  fully  distributed, 
 partially  distributed,  or  non-distributed  midpoints  (M).  These  configurations  are  further  reviewed 
 in  conjunction  with  earthing  approaches  such  as  TN-S  and  IT,  including  the  use  of  single  and 
 multiple  grounding  points.  Combinations  of  polarity,  earthing  scheme,  and  isolation  of  the 
 AC/DC  interlink  are  assessed  to  highlight  practical  implications,  including  stray  currents  and 
 corrosion  risks  resulting  from  earthing  choices  and  system  topology.  Additionally,  the  interaction 
 between  polarity,  earthing,  and  isolation  of  the  AC/DC  interlink  is  analyzed  with  respect  to  fault 
 behavior,  protection  requirements,  electrical  spacing,  personal  safety,  maintenance  and 
 operational considerations. 
 6.1.  Polarities

 significantly  differs  with  respect  to  ground.  These  polarities  have  different  characteristics  and 
 can affect the requirements for the technology or connected equipment used in the system.

 6.1.1.1.  Bipolar with Distributed M 
 This  configuration  uses  a  full  3  conductor  (plus  PE)  distribution.  It  requires  all  3  conductors  (L+, 
 M,  and  L-)  to  be  distributed  throughout  the  system  and  it  provides  full  flexibility  to  use  sources 
 and loads connected in four different configurations: 
 ●  L+ and L- 
 ●  L+, M and L- 
 ●  L+ and M 
 ●  M and L- 
 However,  a  bipolar  system  that  has  sources  or  loads  connected  to  only  one  pole  (L+  to  M,  or  M 
 to  L-),  has  the  potential  to  become  imbalanced  if  the  loading  is  not  well  matched.  The  M 
 conductor  could  be  rated  for  lower  current,  and  using  thinner  cross-section,  but  only  if  it  is 
 ensured  that  the  maximum  unbalance  is  limited  or  M  is  appropriately  protected  against  over 
 current.  This  limits  the  ability  of  the  system  to  continue  operation  on  one  pole  (in  the  event  of  a 
 fault  in  the  other  pole)  to  the  maximum  current  rating  of  the  M  conductor.  The  requirement  that 
 both  poles  are  required  to  trip  in  a  M  grounded  conductor  system  (as  is  currently  required  in 
 NEC standards) can be configured in some protection devices for Bipolar DC grids.

 6.2.1.  Isolation of Electrical Systems 
 Isolation  is  typically  used  when  transitioning  between  systems  with  different  voltage  levels, 
 safety  classes,  or  grounding  requirements.  When  it  comes  to  grounding,  isolation  creates  two 
 separate  systems  which  can  have  separate  grounding  configurations  (in  the  NEC,  the  new 
 system  created  by  isolation  is  defined  as  a  separately  derived  system).  On  the  contrary,  no 
 isolation  means  that  you  can  have  a  single  system  with  a  single  grounding  configuration.  As  an 
 example,  if  a  non-isolated  interlink  (AC/DC)  is  used,  it  means  that  the  AC  side  grounding  point 
 could be used as the DC side grounding. This configuration can be seen in  Figure 19. TN-S-AC  .

 ●  For  isolated  applications,  any  of  the  following  polarity  and  grounding  options  can  be

 ●  The  AC  side  hard  grounding  point  can  be  utilized  also  as  the  grounding  for  a  DC  grid  if  a

 are  connected  and  the  LVAC  network  serves  only  as  an  intermediate  link,  grounding  on 
 the  AC  side  provides  limited  benefit.  In  such  cases,  it  is  preferable  to  ground  the  DC  side 
 instead. 
 ●  For  a  non-isolated  application  operating  at  low-voltage  AC,  if  the  AC  side  is  grounded, 
 the  DC  side  must  be  implemented  as  a  bipolar  DC  system.  A  unipolar  DC  configuration 
 is  not  suitable  because  the  DC  voltage  must  exceed  the  peak  value  of  the  AC  voltage 
 (both positively and negatively) to avoid uncontrolled rectification through the converter. 
 ●  Advantages to non-isolated interlink: simpler, lower cost, less bulky, higher efficiency. 
 ●  Limitations  for  non-isolated  interlink:  Common  mode  currents  (3rd  harmonic)  →  AC  side 
 grounding  requires  consideration  of  harmonic  effects  and  DC  common  mode  current 
 restrictions apply as well.

 6.2.2.2.  Multiple References to Earth 
 For  DC,  multiple  solid  earthing  points  or  system  referencing  conductors  (SRCs)  should  be 
 avoided  because  of  the  risk  of  DC  currents  through  earth  or  other  conductive  structures  that  can 
 cause  corrosion.  On  the  other  hand,  multiple  references  to  earth  can  be  beneficial  for  selective 
 protection  of  the  DC  grid  and  can  increase  resilience  and  continued  partial  operation  after  faults 
 if  multiple  supplies/sources  exist.  This  can  allow  for  new  architectures  to  emerge  which  could 
 optimize  redundancy  in  data  centers.  Additionally,  for  DC,  these  additional  references  to  earth

 can  be  realized  with  System  Referencing  Devices  (SRD)  that  block  DC  but  conduct  fault

 breakers,  this  maximum  fault  current  is  lower  and  can  make  this  option  more  attractive.

 6.2.3.  IT (Ungrounded System or High Impedance Grounded) 
 IT  systems  are  often  described  as  floating  systems  (ungrounded  in  the  NEC,  with  no  intentional 
 connection  to  ground)  or  high  impedance  grounded  systems.  In  either  case,  impedance  is 
 typically  kept  higher  than  100  Ohm/V  for  DC  or  500  Ohm/V  where  non-isolated  from  AC  (per 
 UNECE  R100,  ISO  5474  series,  and  ISO  6469  series).  IT  systems  are  able  to  continue 
 operation  for  a  certain  time  after  a  first  ground  fault.  This  enables  the  operator  to  continue 
 services  while  looking  for  the  cause  of  the  fault,  repair  it,  and  then  resume  normal  operation. 
 Such  a  system  needs  components  that  are  designed  for  the  full  line-to-line  voltage  because  that 
 voltage  appears  when  one  of  the  active,  potential-carrying  conductors  has  that  fault-causing 
 connection  to  ground.  Specifically,  clearance  distances  (air  gaps)  need  to  be  larger  than  in  a 
 grounded  TN  system,  and  protection  devices  could  be  required  in  the  future  to  clear  a  fault,  with 
 one  pole  only,  against  the  full  voltage  (currently  installation  standards  require  overcurrent 
 protection  on  all  poles,  but  this  is  not  needed,  theoretically).  Figure  22  shows  an  IT  system  that 
 has  no  direct  connection  to  ground  on  the  source  (in  this  case,  the  AC/DC  converter)  and  it 
 shows isolation monitoring and symmetrization.

 6.2.3.1.  Direct Touch Protection (with IMD) 
 IT  systems  require  a  monitoring  device  to  detect  when  a  first  fault  to  ground  happens.  An 
 Insulation  Monitoring  Device  (IMD),  is  a  device  commonly  used  to  do  this  function.  Fault  locator 
 devices  are  not  mandatory  but  can  be  useful  to  locate  the  faulty  feeder  or  branch.  While  it  is 
 possible  to  continue  operation  under  a  first  fault,  it  is  not  recommended  to  keep  this  situation  for 
 a  long  period  of  time,  especially  in  the  case  of  single  pole  protection,  as  it  increases  the  risk  of  a 
 second fault and poses a serious threat for touch safety. 
 6.2.3.2.  Safety (Y) Capacitor Limitations 
 Y  capacitors  are  used  for  electromagnetic  interference  filtering  and  are  designed  to  meet  IEC 
 safety  standards  for  touch  safety.  In  smaller  or  low-power  systems,  the  leakage  current  and 
 stored  energy  are  typically  low  enough  that  the  equipment  remains  safe  to  touch.  However,  in 
 larger  systems  with  higher  capacitance  values,  Y  capacitors  can  store  significant  charge,  and 
 during  discharge  this  may  create  a  hazardous  touch  voltage,  posing  a  potential  safety  risk  if  not 
 properly limited or controlled. 
 6.2.4.  Corrosion 
 Electrochemical  corrosion  is  caused  by  currents  at  the  point  where  they  pass  from  a  metal  into 
 an  electrolytic  medium,  e.g.,  concrete  or  soil.  Electro-chemically,  this  process  is  known  as  a 
 partial  anodic  reaction.  For  DC  current  to  cause  corrosion,  a  material-specific  minimum  potential 
 difference  at  that  metal-electrolyte  interface  must  be  exceeded.  EN  50162  specifies  a  threshold 
 of  200  mV  for  steel-reinforced  concrete  [DIN  EN  50162:2005-05  “Protection  against  corrosion 
 by  stray  current  from  direct  current  systems”].  Using  Faraday’s  law,  it  can  be  calculated  that 
 exposing  an  electrolyte  to  a  current  of  1  A  for  one  year  will  cause  the  loss  of  9.13  kg  of  an  iron 
 electrode (often used for providing the earth reference). 
 This  type  of  corrosion  can  occur  in  DC  installations  when  currents  in  the  grounding  system  (PE) 
 unintentionally  –  but  sometimes  unavoidably  –  leave  the  intended  metallic  PE  conductor.  One

 example  is  fastening  (grounded)  metallic  housing  to  the  foundation  and  the  mounting  screws

 7.  Protection and Safety 
 7.1.  Introduction 
 Protection  of  electrical  installations  -including  data  centers-  from  faults,  such  as  short  circuits, 
 arcing,  insulation  or  ground  faults,  is  essential  for  ensuring  safety  and  reliability.  Without 
 protective  measures,  faults  can  lead  to  severe  consequences  for  people,  equipment  and 
 property. 
 When  designing  protection  for  a  data  center  many  aspects  must  be  taken  into  account, 
 including: 
 ●  protecting  against  excess  currents,  that  could  overheat  conductors  and  damage 
 electronic equipment; 
 ●  protecting  humans  from  electric  shock,  electrocution  and  arc  flashes,  that  could  reach 
 extreme temperature and cause severe burns or blindness; 
 ●  preventing  electrical  faults,  including  overcurrent  and  arc  faults,  that  can  evolve  into 
 fires; 
 ●  protecting  sensitive  electronic  equipment  from  high-voltage  disturbances  such  as 
 transient voltage surges; 
 ●  ensuring  continuity  of  service  of  the  data  center  in  case  of  a  fault,  by  quickly  isolating  the 
 faulted  circuit  and  ensuring  that  operation  in  the  rest  of  the  installation  remains 
 unaffected (selectivity); 
 ●  meeting  requirements  of  applicable  codes  and  standards,  including  the  National 
 Electrical Code (NFPA 70) or wiring codes based on IEC 60364 standards. 
 Unlike  AC  power  systems  and  other  DC  systems,  such  as  those  in  railways,  DC  power 
 distribution  systems  for  AI  data  centers  are  characterized  by  relatively  low  inductance  and 
 dominated  by  power-electronic  sources  and  loads,  e.g.  AC/DC  and  DC/DC  power  converters  or 
 MV  solid-state  transformers  (MV-SSTs).  This  changes  the  dynamic  behavior  and  evolution  of 
 the  faults  and,  consequently,  the  requirements  on  circuit  protection.  The  low  inductance  of  DC 
 Date:  30 MARCH, 2026 
 PAGE  86 
 This work is licensed under a  Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike  4.0 International License  . 

===PAGE 87===
 circuits  and  the  contribution  of  DC  bus  capacitors  discharging  into  the  fault  demand  specific 
 protection  strategies  that  act  within  microseconds  to  milliseconds.  Yet  these  challenges  are  well 
 understood and have been effectively addressed through proven engineering solutions. 
 In  addition  to  traditional  protection  devices,  fuses  and  electromechanical  circuit  breakers,  new 
 protection  technologies  -  such  as  fast-acting,  semiconductor  (solid-state)  circuit  breakers  and 
 power  converters  with  overcurrent  blocking  capability  solid-state  protection  devices  -  have  been 
 introduced and have matured tremendously over the years. 
 This  section  provides  a  comprehensive  yet  practical  overview  of  protection  and  safety  in  DC 
 power  distribution  systems,  based  on  best  practices,  real-world  experience,  and  standards, 
 aimed  to  prove  that  DC  power  systems  to  feed  power  hungry  AI  factories  are  safe  and  reliable 
 and no longer a future aspiration, and to serve as a reference for protection design blueprints. 
 7.2.  Protection against overcurrent 
 Overcurrent  faults  considered  in  the  scope  of  this  document  are:  pole-to-pole,  pole-to-midpoint 
 and pole-to-ground, short-circuit and high-impedance faults, including parallel arcs. 
 Protection  from  overcurrent  in  power  systems  is  addressed  in  IEC  60364-4-43  and  in  Sections 
 110  and  240  of  NFPA  70.  As  described  below,  power  converters  play  an  important  role  in  fault 
 protection,  further  investigations  are  needed  if  this  is  properly  considered  in  current  codes  and 
 standards. 
 7.2.1.  Overcurrent faults in active DC systems 
 It  is  assumed  that  in  DC  power  distribution  systems  for  data  centers,  sources  and  loads  are 
 interfaced  to  the  DC  bus  through  power  converters  that  regulate  the  voltage  and  maintain  it  in 
 the  required  control  band.  These  DC  installations  are  characterized  by  capacitive  sources 
 connected  at  a  short  distance  (hence  low  inductivity)  to  the  loads,  in  the  event  of  a  short  circuit 
 the  fault  current  increases  very  rapidly;  an  increase  in  the  order  of  several  tens  of  A/µs  has  been 
 experienced.

 Currently,  no  standards  or  recognized  practices  exist  providing  methods  for  assessing  the  short 
 circuit  current  of  this  kind  of  DC  power  systems;  IEC  TC73  has  recently  started  a  new  project 
 aimed at filling this gap. 
 Considering  a  simple  system  with  only  one  converter,  the  short-circuit  current  exhibits  an  initial 
 peak  resulting  from  the  discharge  of  the  capacitors  followed  by  the  steady-state  contribution 
 from  power  conversion,  which  depends  on  the  type  of  the  converter.  Fault-limiting  converters 
 [Ca]  (see  Section  8.3  )  are  able  to  limit  the  steady-state  stage  of  the  fault  current  to  zero  or  low 
 values  in  the  range  of  the  nominal  current,  not  sufficient  to  sustain  the  voltage  on  the  DC  bus.  If 
 the  fault  is  not  cleared  in  time,  the  voltage  decays  to  values  lower  than  the  minimum  operating 
 voltage  of  the  system;  the  time  to  reach  this  level  depends  on  the  capacitors  and  on  the 
 fault-loop  inductance  and  can  be  as  low  as  a  few  100s  microseconds.  Fast-acting  overcurrent 
 protection  devices  are  therefore  required  to  limit  the  DC  voltage  drop  and  ensure  unaffected 
 parts  of  the  system  continue  operation.  On  the  other  hand,  fault-feeding  converters  (see 
 Section  8.3  )  can  contribute  to  sustain  the  DC  voltage,  but  they  require  an  overcurrent  protection 
 device fast enough to avoid that excess current results in permanent internal damage or fires.

 7.2.2.  Selectivity 
 As  mentioned  above,  protection  is  a  key  aspect  not  only  to  avoid  injury  to  personnel  and 
 damage  to  the  installation  but  also  to  achieve  the  high  system  availability  required  for  data 
 centers.  In  case  of  a  fault,  the  healthy  (fault-free)  part  of  the  system  must,  as  far  as  possible, 
 continue operation. 
 Protection  selectivity  (or  discrimination)  is  a  system’s  ability  to  have  the  circuit  protection  device 
 closest  to  the  fault  interrupt  and  therefore  isolate  only  the  directly  affected  part  of  a  system  in  the 
 event  of  a  fault,  while  ensuring  continued  operation  of  the  fault-free  components  and  sections  as 
 far as possible. 
 Over  the  years,  several  methods  have  been  developed  to  achieve  selectivity  in  AC  distribution 
 systems,  but  Time-Current  Selectivity  remains  the  most  widespread.  This  consists  of  setting 
 protection  devices  to  trip  at  different  time  delays  for  the  same  level  of  overcurrent,  with  the 
 device  closer  to  the  load  set  to  trip  faster  and  the  upstream  device  waiting  longer.  If  the 
 downstream  breaker  clears  the  fault  in  time,  the  upstream  device  does  not  trip.  The  high  inertia 
 of  AC  systems  makes  this  relatively  easy  but  can  result  in  delays  of  up  to  100-200  ms  and, 
 consequently, in case of arc flash, long arcing time and high energy release. 
 In  a  DC  system,  due  to  the  fast  fault  evolution,  in  case  of  a  short  circuit  the  voltage  can  drop 
 quickly.  The  fault  should  be  cleared  before  the  voltage  drops  below  the  minimum  operating 
 voltage  of  the  converter  and  the  system  ceases  to  function,  unless  alternative  strategies  are 
 implemented, as detailed below. 
 Additionally,  with  many  energy  storage  devices  connected  in  parallel,  ensuring  selectivity  is 
 more  challenging  than  in  a  conventional  linear  configuration  with  a  central  power  source,  since 
 in the event of a fault all energy storage devices may discharge into the fault location.

 For  selectivity,  it  must  therefore  be  ensured  that  the  protection  devices  in  unaffected  parts  of  the 
 system  will  not  trip  when  energy  storage  devices,  such  as  capacitors,  feed  current  into  a  fault 
 via  the  DC  grid.  This  can  be  achieved,  for  example,  by  detecting  the  direction  of  current  flow  in 
 the DC breaker. Some possible alternative methods that require further investigation include: 
 ●  use  fast  protection  devices,  e.g.  fast  semiconductor  circuit  breakers  (see  below)  to  avoid 
 the  voltage  drops  below  the  minimum  voltage.  This  is  the  simplest  solution,  but 
 semiconductor  circuit  breakers  are  just  arriving  in  the  market  now  and  the  product 
 portfolio may not cover all needs in the shortest terms. 
 ●  Add  capacitors  to  store  enough  energy  to  meet  the  Voltage  hold-up  time  requirements 
 with  the  breaking  time  of  the  existing  protection  devices.  This  solution  is  based  on 
 mature  protection  technology,  but  the  amount  of  stored  energy  needed  must  be  carefully 
 evaluated to avoid safety and reliability issues. 
 ●  Redundant-feeders  architecture,  in  which  each  rack  is  supplied  by  at  least  two 
 independent  DC  buses,  arranged  in  a  way  that  a  fault  in  a  bus  does  not  propagate  to  the 
 others,  which  can  continue  feeding  the  rack.  This  solution  is  simpler  in  terms  of 
 protection, but it introduces some design constraints that can reduce flexibility. 
 In  any  case,  if  no  intentional  tripping  delay  is  used,  the  arcing  time  in  case  of  arc-flash  may  be 
 limited  to  the  time  necessary  for  the  protection  devices  to  interrupt  instantaneously,  and 
 consequently lower energy release can be expected. 
 7.2.3.  Branch vs supplementary protection 
 NFPA  70  distinguishes  between  overcurrent  protective  devices  based  on  many  factors  including 
 design,  ratings  and  application.  Supplementary  overcurrent  protection  devices  are  considered 
 to  provide  limited  overcurrent  protection  within  equipment,  and  is  only  supplementary  to 
 protection  provided  by  an  overcurrent  protective  device  in  the  distribution  system.  Overcurrent 
 protective  devices  such  as  fuses  and  molded-case  circuit  breakers  (also  referred  to  as  branch 
 circuit  overcurrent  protective  devices),  are  considered  to  provide  protection  to  the  distribution

===PAGE 91===
 system,  which  includes  branch,  feeder  and  service  conductors  and  equipment.  Such  distinction 
 does not exist in IEC standards. 
 An  example  of  supplementary  protection  protective  devices  are  gR  semiconductor  fuses 
 according  to  UL  248-13,  intended  for  protection  of  power  electronic  equipment,  which  are  not 
 suitable  for  branch  protection.  Another  example  may  be  a  hotswap  controller  located  at  the 
 server  shelf  input.  It  protects  the  input  connector  from  inrush  current  and  arcing  damage  during 
 hot-swap  events,  and  it  can  also  function  as  an  electronic  fuse  to  interrupt  the  current  path 
 during severe overcurrent or short-circuit conditions. 
 Requirements  for  branch  circuit  overcurrent  protection  devices  are  typically  more  stringent  than 
 for  supplementary  protection  devices,  e.g.  they  have  higher  minimum  short  circuit  interrupting 
 ratings,  larger  clearance  and  creepage  distances,  and  more  robust  performance  criteria.  NFPA 
 70  specifies  branch  overcurrent  protection  devices  to  be  readily  accessible,  whereas 
 supplementary protection devices can be embedded in the equipment, e.g. in cabinets. 
 An  example  of  a  branch  circuit  overcurrent  protection  device  is  a  circuit  breaker  located  at  a  DC 
 PDU  output  in  the  power  rack  or  sidecar,  or  at  the  input  of  a  DC  bus.  It  shall  be  easily 
 accessible,  provide  a  clear  visual  indication  of  disconnection,  and  support  lockout/tagout 
 (LOTO) for maintenance activities. 
 7.2.4.  Overcurrent protection devices 
 Many  different  overcurrent  protection  devices  have  been  developed  for  DC,  ranging  from 
 traditional  fuses  and  electromechanical  circuit  breakers,  that  operate  by  creating  an  arc  in  the 
 conductive  path  that  develops  an  arc  voltage  larger  than  the  system  voltage,  to  new  equipment 
 based on power semiconductor devices. 
 Every  overcurrent  protection  device  rated  for  the  appropriate  voltage  and  current  levels  is  able 
 to clear overcurrent faults, regardless of technology, thus ensuring safety. 
 According  to  NFPA  70  Section  110.10,  overcurrent  protection  devices  shall  be  coordinated  with 
 the  available  fault  current.  However,  there  is  currently  no  standard  and  no  recognized  practice

 7.2.4.1 Fuses 
 Fuses  need  a  minimum  energy  to  melt  and  interrupt  the  current.  In  datasheets,  this  is  typically 
 stated  in  terms  of  the  Joule  integral  (  I  2  t  )  of  the  current  required  for  melting  the  fuse:  I  2  t  melt  .  In 
 case  the  I  2  t  of  the  fault  (  I  2  t  fault  )  is  lower  than  I  2  t  melt  ,  then  the  fuse  will  not  melt.  In  case  of  fault 
 limiting  converters,  where  the  available  fault  energy  is  stored  in  the  capacitors,  such  capacitors 
 shall  be  large  enough  to  meet  such  criterion  mentioned  [Rav].  As  I  2  t  fault  in  DC  can  depend  on  the 
 shape  of  the  fault  current,  the  trip  time  -and  consequently  the  let-through  current-  of  a  fuse  can 
 vary significantly. 
 In  short-circuit,  fuses  have  an  interruption  time  ranging  from  less  than  1  ms,  for  fast  fuses 
 intended for semiconductor protection (IEC 60269-4, UL 248-13), to several ms. 
 Low-Voltage  fuses  are  covered  by  IEC  60269  and  UL  248  standards.  UL  standards  for  fuses 
 suitable for branch circuit overcurrent protection are currently limited to 600V. 
 7.2.4.2 Electromechanical Circuit breakers 
 Electromechanical  circuit  breakers  according  to  IEC  60947-2,  UL  489  or  UL  1066  are  also 
 suitable  for  DC.  Most  DC  circuit  breakers  in  the  market  have  a  thermal-magnetic  trip  unit  and 
 poles  arranged  in  series  to  build  up  a  large  enough  arc  voltage.  Breaking  time  in  short  circuit 
 ranges  from  about  1  ms,  for  small  miniature  circuit  breakers  (MCBs),  to  a  few  tens  of 
 milliseconds,  for  power  circuit  breakers.  In  active  DC  systems,  breaking  time  is  dominated  by 
 mechanical inertia, as short arcing time can be expected. 
 In  case  of  active  DC  systems  fed  by  fault  limiting  converters,  the  thermal-magnetic  trip  unit  may 
 not  fit  very  well  with  the  fault  behavior,  as  the  capacitor-discharge  peak  can  be  too  narrow  to 
 trigger  the  magnetic  protection,  and  the  low  fault  energy  can  result  in  very  long  tripping  time  or 
 no  trip  at  all.  Electronic  trip  units  would  fit  much  better  for  this  application,  but  very  few  products

 are  available  in  the  market.  Electronic  trip  units  can  also  implement  additional  protections,  e.g. 
 Ground-fault protection of equipment (GFPE). 
 7.2.4.3 Solid state CBs (SSCBs), Semiconductor Hybrid CBs (SHCBs) 
 and e-fuses 
 Solid  state,  or  semiconductor,  circuit  breakers  (SSCBs  or  SCCBs),  according  to  the  new  UL 
 489I  or  to  IEC  60947-10,  to  be  published  soon,  use  semiconductor  switching  devices,  e.g. 
 IGBTs,  IGCTs  or  MOSFETs,  in  the  main  current  path,  to  interrupt  current,  instead  of  an  arc. 
 They  include  mechanical  contacts  to  ensure  galvanic  isolation,  as  required  for  safe 
 maintenance, and typically have an electronic trip unit. 
 In  a  short  circuit  condition,  these  devices  have  breaking  time  in  the  order  of  microseconds.  By 
 clearing  the  fault  in  the  early  phase  of  the  capacitor  discharge,  they  limit  the  voltage  drop  even 
 in  installations  with  limited  capacitance.  Due  to  such  ultrafast  interruption,  SSCBs  are  also  able 
 to  break  very  high  prospective  short-circuit  currents.  As  such,  the  key  performance  indicator  is 
 not  the  breaking  capacity  (Ics  /  Icu  in  IEC  60947-2,  or  kAIC  in  UL  489),  but  the  maximum  rate  of 
 rise  of  the  fault  current  (di/dt)  that  the  circuit  breaker  can  interrupt  safely.  Depending  on  the 
 design, SSCBs can break currents up to several hundreds A/µs. 
 However,  one  drawback  is  that  these  devices  have  larger  power  loss  compared  to 
 electromechanical  circuit  breakers  and  may  require  forced-air  or  even  liquid  cooling,  for  the 
 larger current ratings, which could make integration in electrical panels more complex. 
 Semiconductor  hybrid  circuit  breakers  (SCHCBs),  also  covered  by  IEC  60947-10  and  UL  489I, 
 are  similar  to  SSCBs  but,  in  addition  to  the  isolation  contacts,  they  have  mechanical  by-pass 
 contacts  in  parallel  to  the  semiconductor  switching  device,  intended  for  conducting  the  current  in 
 normal  operation  and  therefore  reducing  power  loss.  SCHCBs  have  power  losses  similar  to 
 electromechanical  circuit  breakers  but,  due  to  the  inertia  of  the  by-pass  contacts,  they  are  much 
 slower  than  SSCBs;  their  typical  breaking  time  is  in  the  order  of  a  few  hundreds  µs,  making 
 them suitable for currents with a rate of rise up to some A/µs. 
 Also  similar  to  SSCBs,  but  without  integral  isolation  contacts,  are  solid-state  overcurrent 
 protectors,  also  called  e-Fuses  in  the  industry.  They  have  breaking  time  in  the  same  order  as

 7.3.  Protection against electric shock 
 Requirements  regarding  protection  against  electric  shock  in  electrical  installations  are  provided 
 by IEC 60364-4-41. 
 Subclause  411.3.2  specifies  requirements  for  automatic  disconnection  in  case  of  fault, 
 applicable  also  in  DC.  Requirements  depend  on  the  grounding  system  and  the  type  of  interlink 
 converter.  In  TN-S  systems  with  isolated  interlink  converters,  a  protective  device  shall 
 automatically  disconnect  and  isolate  all  the  supplies  to  the  line  conductor  in  the  event  of  a 
 low-impedance  fault  between  the  line  conductor  and  an  exposed  conductive-part  or  a  protective 
 conductor  in  the  circuit  or  equipment.  According  to  Table  41.1  of  IEC  60364-4-41,  for  voltage 
 higher than 400 VDC, the disconnection time shall not exceed: 
 ●  0.1  s,  for  final  (branch)  circuits  with  rated  current  up  to  63  A,  if  including  one  or  more 
 socket-outlets, and up to 32 A if supplying only fixed connected equipment; 
 ●  5  s,  for  distribution  circuits  (feeders)  and  for  final  circuits  other  than  those  mentioned 
 above. 
 The  same  requirement  applies  to  systems  with  non-isolated  interlink  converters,  with  grounded 
 AC  neutral  and  DC  active  parts  ungrounded  (see  Section  6.2.1.3  ).  For  IT  systems,  including 
 both  ungrounded  and  impedance-ground  ed  sy  stems,  IEC  60364-4-41  does  not  mandate 
 automatic  disconnection  on  the  occurrence  of  a  first  fault,  provided  a  residual  current  monitor 
 (RCM)  or  an  insulation  monitoring  device  (IMD)  is  used  to  signal  the  occurrence  of  such  first 
 fault  with  an  audible  and/or  visual  signal  (IEC  60364-4-41,  411.6.3.1).  On  the  other  hand,  NFPA 
 70  does  require  capability  to  detect  a  first  fault  if  the  IT  system  is  ungrounded  (no  intentional 
 connection to ground through high impedance) in Section 250.167(A). 
 For  grounding  systems  different  from  IT,  subclause  411.3.3  of  IEC  60364-4-41  also  mandates 
 use  of  a  residual  current  protective  device  (RCD)  with  residual  current  of  30  mA  to  provide 
 additional  protection  of  AC  sockets  (receptacles)  with  rated  current  up  to  32  A  used  by  ordinary 
 persons,  for  applications  requiring  special  attention,  e.g.  in  kitchen  and  bathrooms,  lighting 
 systems,  outdoor  installations  and  eV-charging.  Additional  protection  for  DC  systems  is  under 
 consideration.

 7.4.  Protection against thermal effects and ﬁres 
 Insulation  failures,  ground  faults  and  series-arc  faults  in  power  distribution  systems,  may  evolve 
 into  large  parallel  arcs,  resulting  in  electrical  fires  and  arc-flash  hazard.  These  risks  need  to  be 
 considered and further evaluated. 
 IEC  60364-4-42  sets  out  protective  measures  for  locations  where  consequences  of  fire  are 
 severe,  which  include  locations  where  people  sleep  (e.g.  in  dwellings  or  hotels),  or  intended  for 
 groups  of  persons,  or  museums,  or  others.  Such  measures  are  the  same  for  AC  and  DC 
 systems and include: 
 ●  for  TN-S  and  IT  grounding:  installing  an  RCD  (or  GFPE)  with  a  rated  residual  operating 
 current not exceeding 300 mA (for non resistive loads); 
 ●  for  IT  grounding  and  isolated  interlink  converter:  installing  an  insulation  monitoring 
 device  (IMD)  monitoring  the  whole  installation,  and  providing  measures  to  eliminate  a 
 first earth fault with the shortest practicable delay 
 While  not  mandatory,  these  measures  should  be  considered  for  data  centers  due  to  the  high 
 economic value of these. 
 For  power  distribution  to  industrial  and  data  center  loads,  NFPA  70  indirectly  addresses  a 
 specific  type  of  ground  and  parallel-arc  faults,  where  those  faults  have  low  impedance  to 
 ground.  This  is  done  for  AC  through  the  requirements  of  several  sections,  including  Section 
 210.13(A),  and  has  recently  been  extended  to  DC  through  Section  210.13(B)  and  correlating 
 updates  to  other  sections  as  well,  requiring  branch-protection  disconnecting  means  rated  1000A 
 or  more,  installed  in  solidly  grounded  DC  systems  at  voltage  up  to  1500  VDC,  to  provide 
 ground-  fault  protection  of  equipment  (GFPE).  For  AC,  this  is  supported  by  the  UL  1053  listing 
 and for DC this is under investigation by UL.

 7.5.  Protection against voltage disturbances 
 Protection  against  voltage  disturbances  is  addressed  in  Article  242  of  NFPA  70  and  IEC

 characteristics  of  the  circuit.  Protection  devices  based  on  semiconductors  (SSCBs,  SCHCBs 
 and  e-fuses)  typically  include  surge  protection  devices  (SPDs)  to  limit  switching  overvoltage. 
 IEC  60947-10  for  SSCBs  and  SCHCBs,  and  IEC  60269-3  for  semiconductor  fuses  require  the

 7.6.  Protection against arc-ﬂash 
 There  is  the  perception  that  the  trend  towards  higher  power  densities  in  AI  factories  results  in  a 
 significant increase of fire and arc-flash risks. 
 A  single  arc-flash  incident  can  cause  injury  or  death  to  personnel  and  destroy  entire  rows  of 
 expensive servers and other equipment, costing millions dollars . 
 Whenever  possible,  no  work  should  be  done  on  energized  equipment.  However,  in  data  centers, 
 typically  operating  on  a  24/7/365  basis  with  strict  uptime  guarantees,  turning  off  the  power  in  a 
 room  or  in  a  line  of  racks  is  impractical.  As  such,  technicians  may  be  required  to  perform 
 maintenance and upgrade works on equipment while it is still energized. 
 For  this  reason,  assessing  and  mitigating  arc  flash  risks  is  crucial,  in  particular  the  adoption  of 
 adequate  personal  protection  equipment  (PPE),  as  described  in  NFPA  70E,  protecting  the 
 workers  thermal  injuries  resulting  from  the  energy  release  or  electric  arcs  (arc  flash).  This 
 requires the Incident energy (  IE  m  ) of the arc to be  calculated. 
 New  Section  110.16  of  NFPA  70  2026  also  requires  permanent  arc-flash  hazard  labels  on 
 distribution  equipment  likely  to  require  servicing,  or  maintenance  while  energized.  Additionally, 
 Sections  240.67  and  240.87  of  NFPA  70  require  mitigation  strategies  be  employed  for  circuits 
 with overcurrent protective devices rated 1200 amperes or higher.

 for  AC.  However,  NFPA  70E  Annex  D.5  provides  recognized  methods  based  on  different 
 studies,  one  of  which  is  the  “Maximum  Power  Method”  [D.5.1],  known  to  give  conservative  IE  m

 has  become  significant.  Grid  disturbances  involving  data  centers  have  occurred  across  North

 8.2.  On-Site Generation 
 As  AI-driven  applications  scale,  technology  companies  have  traditionally  relied  on  the  local  grid 
 to  power  their  data  centers.  But  with  soaring  demand,  the  grid  is  struggling  to  keep  up,  facing 
 capacity  and  transmission  constraints.  As  a  result,  data  center  developers—who  typically  focus 
 on  facility  design  and  optimization—  are  increasingly  looking  for  alternative  power  solutions  that 
 are reliable and scalable. 
 On-site  generation  can  function  as  behind-the-meter,  island  microgrid  solutions,  enabling  data 
 centers to operate independently where grid infrastructure is insufficient or unavailable. 
 In  locations  where  grid  operators  require  onsite  power  to  contribute  to  the  electricity  market, 
 these  technologies  can  also  run  in  parallel  with  the  grid  as  dispatchable  resources,  providing 
 additional flexibility. 
 In  the  case  of  on-site  power  generation,  the  applicable  requirements  for  grid  interconnection 
 when grid is the source of power, and described in the previous chapter, are no longer needed .

 ●  A  direct  influence  of  AC/DC  conversion  harmonics  onto  the  microgrid  power  quality: 
 higher reactive power, losses, frequency and voltage stability; 
 ●  The  reduced  ability  of  the  on-site  generation  to  immediately  react  to  the  AI  workload 
 oscillations,  given  the  more  limited  generation  capacity  compared  to  having  the  whole 
 grid as a source.

 Fault current behavior can be split into 2 categories: 
 1.  Fault feeding (converter type 1,2 and 3) 
 In  this  category,  the  fault  current  from  the  AC  source  to  the  DC  distribution  cannot  be  limited.

 semiconductor  switch)  embedded  which  will  limit  the  fault  current  in  duration  and  or  in  peak

 current (e.g 1.5x, 2x) and limit in duration (e.g 100ms, 200ms). 
 It  is  worth  noticing  that  in  any  case,  there  could  be  a  short  surge  current  when  the  fault  happens 
 because of the exposed capacitor of the converter on the DC side.

 8.4.  ESS 
 Energy  storage  systems  (ESS)  are  becoming  critical  components  of  modern  data  center 
 infrastructure.  They  go  beyond  the  traditional  role  of  an  Uninterruptible  Power  Supply  (UPS)  by 
 offering  fast  stabilization  for  AI-driven  load  swings,  reliable  ride-through  during  grid 
 disturbances, cost optimization, renewable integration, and grid services. 
 The  figure  here  under  represents  applications  by  the  time  frame  the  storage  is  expected  to  be 
 active:  milliseconds  for  local  power  quality,  seconds  for  rapid  stabilization  and  ride-through, 
 minutes  for  UPS  bridging  and  peak  shaving,  and  hours  for  energy  arbitrage,  renewable  shifting, 
 and extended backup: 
 Figure 30. Power Equipment Applications 
 The key functions of ESS in a data center: 
 1.  Smoothing power swings (milliseconds to seconds) 
 AI  workloads  present  high  power  fluctuation,  ramping  power  from  30%  to  100%  in  milliseconds 
 [Choukse  et  al.  2025].  These  ramps  and  spikes  are  characterized  by  different  dynamic  elements 
 occurring  at  different  timescales  (milliseconds,  seconds,  minutes),  thus  stressing  equipment  and 
 degrading  overall  power  quality.  Without  mitigation,  the  result  can  be  voltage  sags,  oscillations, 
 threaten  broader  grid  stability,  and  risk  tripping  or  damaging  nearby  generation  and  other 
 upstream  equipment.  Energy  storage  systems  address  these  dynamics  by  rapidly  absorbing  or 
 injecting  power  across  the  relevant  timescales,  smoothing  ramps  and  spikes,  damping 
 oscillations, and maintaining stable voltage and frequency through coordinated controls.

 2.  Providing backup power during grid disturbance (seconds  to minutes) 
 This  is  the  most  fundamental  function.  Data  centers  require  uninterrupted  power  for  their 
 mission-critical servers and other systems. 
 Traditionally,  backup  of  the  facility  is  by  UPS,  and  battery  backup  units  (BBUs)  are  also 
 deployed  in  or  close  to  IT  racks.  They  ensure  that  the  data  center  remains  operational  during 
 grid  failures  or  until  backup  generators  can  be  brought  online.  The  timescales  required  for  these 
 types  of  operations  are  generally  considered  to  be  minutes.  Alternative  solutions  could  be  used, 
 for  example,  ESS  with  extended  runtime  to  even  replace  generators  entirely  in  some  cases.  By 
 using  batteries  (for  example)  instead  of  diesel  generators  for  backup,  data  centers  can  achieve 
 a  zero-emission  backup  solution,  supporting  sustainability  goals.  The  size  and  cost  of  the 
 battery  will  be  directly  proportional  to  the  amount  of  time  that  a  given  power  level  is  desired  to 
 be provided. 
 Moreover,  during  Post-fault  active  power  recovery  the  data  center  must  be  able  to  quickly 
 reconnect  to  the  grid  after  a  disturbance  is  cleared.  Some  grid  operators  require  large  loads  to 
 return  to  roughly  90–100%  of  pre-fault  power  consumption  within  about  one  second.  ESS 
 enables  this  by  “mimicking”  the  load  for  the  grid,  absorbing  power  during  the  reconnection  ramp 
 to keep the facility’s net import near its pre-fault level until the grid stabilizes. 
 3.  Grid Services and Energy Optimization (minutes to  hours) 
 In  many  regions  there  is  a  paid  market  for  grid  services  (e.g.,  frequency  regulation  and 
 reserves),  while  in  others  the  emphasis  is  on  strict  grid-code  compliance;  ESS  can  support  both, 
 optimizing  economics  where  markets  exist  and  ensuring  reliable,  compliant  operation 
 everywhere. 
 ●  Energy  arbitrage:  store  low ‑ cost  electricity  and  discharge  when  prices  are  high  to 
 optimize energy costs. 
 ●  Distribution  efficiency:  smooth  power  profiles  to  keep  electrical  and  computing 
 systems in efficient operating regions and reduce losses.

 8.4.2  ESS Safety 
 ESS  safety  is  a  complex  subject.  The  intent  here  is  to  provide  a  high  level  guide  to  ensure 
 awareness  of  the  most  critical  aspects  and  some  understanding  of  why  they  should  be 
 considered.  Most  any  ESS  technology  or  medium  poses  at  least  some  kind  of  thermal  risk.  Any 
 ESS  that  utilizes  an  electrolyte  consisting  of  an  organic  carbonate  solvent  will,  in  a  failure 
 scenario,  produce  an  offgas  that  poses  a  risk  for  flammability  and/or  explosion.  This  summary 
 will  discuss  primarily  in  the  context  of  lithium-ion  batteries,  most  electrochemical  storage 
 technologies  (particularly  those  using  organic  carbonate  solvent  electrolytes)  will  be  more 
 similar  than  different.  However,  it  is  important  to  note  that  some  lithium-ion  batteries  decrease  or 
 completely  mitigate  some  of  these  risks  through  the  use  of  different  materials  (aqueous 
 electrolytes,  ceramic  separators,  oxide-based  anodes),  making  them  safer  and  more  suitable  for 
 high-risk  or  mission  critical  applications.  Capacitors  typically  present  lower  or  no  fire  risk  (ie 
 ceramic),  but  technologies  using  electrolytes  (electrolytic,  supercapacitors)  do  constitute  thermal 
 risk.  In  most  cases,  thermal  events  are  characterized  by  very  low  likelihood  of  occurrence,  but 
 the  impacts  of  an  event  can  be  so  severe  that  the  overall  risk  profile  demands  attention.  The 
 risks  and  results  of  thermal  events  can  be  managed  and  mitigated  with  existing  engineering 
 tools  and  technologies.  But  variations  exist  between  different  technologies  and  form  factors 
 such  that  the  mitigating  solutions  must  be  fit  for  purpose.  This  poses  a  challenge  for  ongoing 
 efforts  towards  standardization  and  for  early  stage  project  scoping.  In  addition,  human  error  is 
 often found to be the failure mode behind many historical ESS safety events. 
 Standards  and  best  practices  for  large  outdoor  ESS  installations  do  exist  with  years  of  history. 
 However,  the  prospect  of  a  significant  amount  of  energy  storage  capacity  distributed  throughout 
 an  indoor  space  poses  new  and  different  challenges.  The  following  summaries  discuss  general 
 aspects  of  ESS  safety  for  consideration,  but  primarily  in  the  context  of  challenges  and  needs  for 
 those indoor installations. 
 1.  Thermal  runaway:  A  self-accelerating  thermal  event  that  is  characterized  by  exothermic 
 processes.  Any  medium  or  technology  that  stores  chemical  energy  is  capable  of  and  at 
 Date:  30 MARCH, 2026 
 PAGE  120 
 This work is licensed under a  Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike  4.0 International License  . 

===PAGE 121===
 risk  of  some  degree  of  a  thermal  event.  However,  the  amount  of  thermal  energy  released 
 and  the  extent  of  abuse  that  is  required  before  forcing  a  cell  into  thermal  runaway 
 certainly  varies  in  different  chemistries.  However,  suggestions  that  some  battery 
 technologies  are  completely  incapable  of  a  thermal  event  should  be  considered  with 
 skepticism.  Some  technologies  may  produce  benign  responses  to  certain  failure  modes, 
 but  still  be  at  risk  of  significant  thermal  events  in  response  to  other  failure  modes. 
 Objective  testing  and  valid  data  is  critical  for  assessment  of  the  true  risks,  capabilities 
 and  requirements.  Generally,  a  single  thermal  event  (i.e.  in  a  single  cell)  is  of  relatively 
 mild  consequence  to  the  overall  facility.  The  primary  threat  and  challenge  is  the  likely 
 scenario  that  this  single  cell  event  will  propagate  to  other  cells,  and  thus  spread 
 exponentially.  This  is  a  critical  aspect  of  ESS  system  design  and  the  requirements  are 
 particular  to  the  chemistry  and  module/system  design  and  form  factor.  Evaluating 
 thermal and propagation behaviors is a core component of UL 9540a testing. 
 2.  Electrical:  a  great  deal  of  consideration  is  given  to  thermal  aspects  of  ESS  safety. 
 However,  practically  speaking,  the  vast  majority  of  risk  and  real  incidences  are  related  to 
 electrical  shock  and  arc  flash.  Batteries  present  voltages  often  over  1,000  VDC  and, 
 critically,  are  always  live.  Proper  precaution,  processes  and  equipment  for  aspects  such 
 as  disconnect  and  isolation,  particularly  during  installation  and  service,  are  critical  to 
 ensuring safety. 
 3.  Ventilation:  In  the  case  of  a  thermal  event,  offgas  will  be  released  that  is  both  flammable 
 and  explosive.  Managing  and  ducting  this  gas  to  avoid  an  explosion  is  a  key  challenge  - 
 particularly  for  indoor  installations.  There  are  many  different  approaches  that  can  be 
 considered  for  mitigating  risks  -  appropriateness  depends  on  the  technology,  form  factor, 
 and  the  space  within  which  it  is  installed.  Ventilation  poses  one  of  the  most  substantial 
 challenges  with  regard  to  infrastructure  and  available  space,  and  is  one  of  the  required 
 safety systems that is often forgotten until the end. 
 4.  Fire  suppression:  Generally  speaking,  putting  out  a  lithium  ion  battery  fire  is  difficult  and 
 access  directly  to  the  cell  in  thermal  runaway  is  typically  not  possible.  Thus,

 PAGE  121

===PAGE 122===
 ‘extinguishing  the  fire’  is  not  a  typical  objective.  A  key  objective  is  to  limit  propagation,  as 
 described  earlier;  and  for  this,  removal  of  heat  is  critical.  Many  different  fire  suppression 
 mediums  exist,  and  each  accels  in  certain  areas  and  may  underperform  in  others. 
 Gaseous  mediums  excel  at  extinguishing  flames,  but  not  at  removing  heat  and  must 
 take  into  account  interactions  with  ventilation.  Water  is  ideal  for  removing  heat  but 
 produces  short  circuit  risks.  Many  others  face  challenges  of  physical  installation 
 feasibility.  The  system  should  be  selected  and  engineered  appropriately  for  the  ESS 
 technology and form factor that is being installed. 
 5.  Codes  and  Standards:  ESS  codes  and  standards  are  continually  evolving.  The 
 differences  between  technologies  and  formulations  makes  it  particularly  difficult  for  ‘one 
 size  fits  all’  safety  requirements.  For  commercial  installations  in  the  US,  the  most 
 relevant  standards  which  will  govern  ESS  installation  are  UL  1973,  UL  9540,  UL  9540a 
 and  NFPA  855.  UL  1973  has  been  the  longest  standing  ESS  fire  safety  standard.  UL 
 9540  addresses  requirements  for  the  ESS  product.  UL  9540a  presents  a  testing  regime 
 which  elicits  the  behaviors  of  what  happens  when  a  given  ESS  fails.  NFPA  855  governs 
 site  requirements  for  the  installation  of  an  ESS  at  a  location,  such  as  sizing,  proximity, 
 spacing.  Given  the  evolving  nature  of  the  ESS  standards  landscape,  in  all  cases  the 
 Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ) will be a driving force for what is required.

 Conditions  for  Normal  Operation  and  under  Failure  conditions  must  be  defined.  One  important 
 Normal  Operation  Condition  to  be  defined  is  ability  or  non-ability  for  disconnect  under  load. 
 While  disconnect  under  load  is  technically  feasible  via  hybrid  electronic  switching  or  other

 11.  Codes and Standards 
 As  direct  current  (DC)  system  architectures  and  microgrids  gain  momentum,  especially  in  AI 
 data  centers  where  efficiency  and  reliability  are  critical  for  high-density  computing,  the  role  of 
 global codes and standards (e.g., UL, IEC, IEEE, etc.) becomes increasingly important. 
 These  codes  and  standards,  along  with  the  testing  and  certification  protocols  and  demonstration 
 of  compliance  that  they  empower,  help  enable  installers,  integrators,  and  operators  to  design 
 and deploy safe, efficient, and interoperable DC systems. 
 However,  the  rapid  growth  of  DC  technologies  is  outpacing  the  standardization  landscape, 
 creating notable challenges. 
 This  section  of  the  white  paper  outlines  the  current  landscape  as  well  as  gaps  in  the  installation 
 codes  and  standards  for  DC  products  and  systems,  explains  why  they  exist,  and  outlines  where 
 industry cooperation is urgently needed. 
 This  first  draft  is  an  initial  –  and  surely  incomplete  –  accounting  of  the  codes  and  standards  that 
 will  support  the  transition  to  a  higher  voltage  DC  future.  By  identifying  these  challenges  early, 
 stakeholders  can  participate  and  accelerate  safe  innovation  and  help  shape  the  next  generation 
 of DC codes and standards. 
 11.1.  Why DC Codes & Standards Matter 
 ●  Safety  :  Clear  regulations  ensure  that  protection  systems,  fault  management,  and 
 grounding schemes are consistently implemented. 
 ●  Interoperability  :  Harmonized  standards  allow  components  from  different  vendors  to 
 work seamlessly together. 
 ●  Scalability  &  Future  Expansion  :  standardization  simplifies  system  replication  and 
 modular  growth,  allowing  capacity,  voltage  levels,  or  new  loads  to  be  added  with  minimal 
 redesign, reduced risk, and lower costs over time. 
 ●  Efficiency & Sustainability  : Proper DC design can  reduce conversion losses. 
 ●  Regulatory  Compliance  :  Standards  enable  local  code  and  regulatory  compliance, 
 which helps streamline installation and commissioning.

 11.2.  Key Challenges in DC Standardization 
 1)  Alternating  Current  (AC)  has  a  100-Year  Head  Star  t:  AC  systems  have 
 benefited  from  decades  of  evolution,  research,  and  global  harmonization.  In 
 contrast,  DC  is  still  emerging  at  scale  –  particularly  at  power  levels  relevant  to 
 data centers. 
 2)  Demand  for  DC  Is  Growing  Faster  Than  Standards  Can  Evolve  :  Industries 
 want  DC  solutions  now  to  meet  efficiency  and  sustainability  goals.  But  many 
 DC-related standards are incomplete, outdated, or not globally aligned. 
 3)  Slow  Standard  Development  Cycles  :  Creating  or  updating  standards  typically 
 takes  several  years,  involving  expert  committees,  testing  cycles,  and 
 multinational  coordination.  This  timeline  does  not  match  the  pace  of  DC 
 innovation  and  requires  urgent  action  from  Standards  Development 
 Organizations (SDOs) to accelerate the pace of requirements. 
 4)  Alignment  Between  International  Standard  Development  Organizations 
 (SDOs)  :  Harmonization  efforts  between  major  bodies  such  as  UL,  IEC  and  IEEE 
 are  ongoing  but  slow.  Misalignment  leads  to  duplicated  testing,  engineering 
 workarounds, and higher costs. 
 5)  Country ‑ Specific  Requirements  :  Installation  codes  and  regulations  differ  widely 
 across  regions,  making  global  deployment  complex.  An  installation  compliant  in 
 Europe may require significant redesign to meet US or Chinese requirements. 
 6)  Cultural  &  Technical  Inertia  :  The  industry  is  largely  built  around  AC.  Many 
 professionals  are  reluctant  to  leave  this  comfort  zone,  creating  skill  gaps  and 
 hesitancy  in  adopting  DC-based  architectures.  Training  and  education  on  DC 
 systems will take time to implement and ensure workers are prepared. 
 7)  Utility  Interface  :  While  the  white  paper  primarily  focuses  on  chip-to-grid  power 
 architecture,  the  point  of  common  coupling  (PCC)  –  where  the  data  center  meets 
 the  utility  –  presents  significant  integration  hurdles.  Data  centers  are  not  "static" 
 loads;  they  are  dynamic,  high-density  environments  that  strain  traditional  grid

 11.3.  System-Level Standards 
 11.3.1.  Existing system-level knowledge and applications 
 Multiple  industries  are  already  piloting,  deploying,  or  operating  DC  microgrids  at  scale.  These 
 sectors  provide  valuable  real ‑ world  experience  that  the  data  center  ecosystem  can  leverage  to 
 accelerate adoption, reduce risk, and lower cost. 
 Industrial  Applications  :  As  outlined  in  previous  sections  of  the  white  paper,  factories, 
 robotics,  process  automation,  and  traction  increasingly  use  DC  to  improve  efficiency, 
 stabilize  renewable  integration,  and  simplify  power  conversion.  These  systems  can  offer 
 insights into coordinated control, protection strategies, and energy flow management. 
 Renewables  :  Renewable  energy  sources  like  solar  photovoltaics  (PV)  and  fuel  cells 
 natively  generate  DC,  and  while  wind  turbines  initially  produce  AC,  their  output  is  often 
 rectified  to  DC  for  efficient  control  and  storage.  In  modern  applications,  DC-coupled 
 systems are emerging as the industry standard for co-located solar and battery storage. 
 Marine  &  Offshore  :  Ships  and  offshore  platforms  rely  heavily  on  DC  grids  to  optimize 
 propulsion  efficiency  and  manage  distributed  energy  sources.  Marine  applications 
 demonstrate  mature  approaches  to  system-level  coordination  and  fault  handling  in 
 compact, mission ‑ critical environments. 
 EV  Charging  Infrastructure  :  In  some  regions,  high ‑ power  EV  fast  chargers  operate 
 natively  on  DC,  and  large  charging  hubs  use  DC  distribution  for  improved  efficiency  and 
 simplified  conversion.  Their  experience  with  high ‑ current  protection,  conversion 
 technologies,  and  the  knowledge  and  learnings  from  AC/DC  interfacing  should  be 
 evaluated for transferability to data centers.

 11.3.3.  System-level gaps and standards in development 
 While  the  individual  components  for  DC  power  distribution  are  maturing  rapidly,  the  industry  is 
 navigating  a  governance  gap  regarding  how  these  products  function  as  a  unified,  resilient 
 ecosystem.  Rapid  mobilization  is  required  to  address  critical  system-level  blind  spots  that,  if  left 
 unmanaged,  could  delay  the  scaling  of  AI-ready  power  distribution  infrastructure.  This  effort 
 focuses  on  moving  beyond  component  safety  to  establish  a  holistic  framework  for  architecture, 
 control  logic,  and  personnel  protection.  By  codifying  these  emerging  practices,  the  industry  aims 
 to replace custom, one-off engineering with repeatable, certified system designs. 
 ●  System  Design  Principles  :  There  is  no  unified  guidance  on  how  to  architect  a  complete 
 DC  system  and  ongoing  strategies  are  in  development  optimal  distribution  topologies, 
 fault management strategies, grounding concepts, and coordinated protection schemes. 
 ●  System  Control  &  Coordination  :  Unlike  AC  systems,  where  frequency  and  voltage 
 behavior  are  well  defined,  DC  systems  rely  on  control  algorithms  and  coordinated

 11.3.5.  Installation Codes: Existing & Gaps 
 DC  power  distribution  in  data  centers  is  governed  by  a  combination  of  regional-  and/or 
 country-level  electrical  safety  codes,  fire  protection  standards,  and  industry  best  practices. 
 Applicable  commissioning  standards  should  be  consulted  and  need  to  be  met  for  each 
 region/installation location. 
 For  electrical  installations  and  safety-related  aspects,  there  are  two  main  Standards 
 Development  Organizations:  International  Electrotechnical  Commission  (IEC)  and  Underwriters 
 Laboratories  (UL).  Whereas  UL  standards  target  primarily  the  United  States  and  Canada  (North 
 American  markets),  IEC  standards  target  global  markets,  especially  Europe  and  Asia.  In 
 addition  to  the  difference  in  the  geographical  scope,  IEC  standards  focus  on  minimum  safety 
 and  performance  requirements  for  electrical  products,  while  UL  Standards  offer  stricter,  more 
 detailed  descriptions  and  rigorously  focus  on  in-depth  product  testing,  factory  inspections,  and 
 follow-up audits. 
 This  white  paper  provides  a  summary  of  global  IEC-based  installation  requirements  below,  while 
 the  US  is  covered  in  more  specific  detail.  Deeper  work  is  required  to  provide  a  comprehensive 
 global  analysis  on  the  current  and  emerging  code  and  regulatory  landscape.  This  will  be 
 covered in a future revision of this document.

 operation at or below 1000 VAC or 1500 VDC. 
 Most  countries  outside  the  United  States  (US)  use  IEC  60364  as  the  base  for  installation  rules 
 with local translation and modifications.

 IEC  Technical  Committee  64  (IEC  TC  64)  is  responsible  for  the  IEC  60364  series,  the 
 international  "gold  standard"  for  low-voltage  electrical  installations.  These  rules  provide  a 
 comprehensive  framework  for  the  design,  erection,  and  verification  of  electrical  systems  to

 This  applies  to  installations  with  nominal  voltages  up  to  1000VAC  or  1500VDC.  The  installation 
 rules are organized into several key parts: 
 ●  Part  1:  Fundamental  Principles  –  Defines  the  scope,  basic  safety  requirements,  and

 ●  Part 4: Protection for Safety  – This is the "safety  engine" of the rules, covering: 
 ○  Electric  Shock  :  Requirements  for  automatic  disconnection  of  supply,  insulation, 
 and the use of Residual Current Devices (RCDs). 
 ○  Thermal Effects  : Rules to prevent fires caused by  electrical equipment. 
 ○  Overcurrent  : Protection against short circuits and  overloads. 
 ○  Voltage  Disturbances  :  Protection  against  surges  and  electromagnetic 
 interference. 
 ●  Part  5:  Selection  and  Erection  of  Equipment  –  Guidance  on  choosing  and  installing 
 wiring systems, switchgear, and earthing arrangements. 
 ●  Part  6:  Verification  –  Specifies  mandatory  initial  inspections  and  periodic  testing  to 
 confirm the installation remains safe over time. 
 ●  Part  7:  Special  Installations  –  Tailored  rules  for  high-risk  locations  like  bathrooms, 
 swimming pools, medical facilities, and solar photovoltaic (PV) systems. 
 ●  Part  8:  Functional  Aspects  –  Modern  additions  focusing  on  energy  efficiency  and 
 "prosumer" installations (where buildings both consume and produce energy).

 11.4.  Interim Guidance 
 DC  technology  is  advancing  faster  than  system-  and  product-level  standards  can  adapt.  While 
 many  product  standards  are  available,  and  some  system  standards  are  emerging,  guidelines  for 
 designing, controlling, maintaining, and integrating DC systems remain under development. 
 Where  gaps  in  standards  or  regulation  exist,  OEMs  can  still  prepare  and  mitigate  risk  by  taking 
 thoughtful  steps  to  help  assure  the  safety  and  performance  of  new  technologies  and  end-use 
 applications. 
 ●  Evaluate  Existing  Standards  –  Review  standards,  OOIs,  or  CRDs  where  a  close 
 alignment  to  the  technology  and  risks  can  be  demonstrated.  For  example,  standards 
 written  for  high-power  DC  systems  in  photovoltaic  (PV)  and  electric  vehicle  (EV) 
 applications can be evaluated for learnings, risks, and testing considerations. 
 ●  Hazard-Based  Risk  Assessment  –  Engage  a  certifying  body  and  use  a  hazard  based 
 risk  assessment  approach  to  conduct  pre-certification  validation,  interim  testing,  or  field 
 evaluation. 
 ●  Modeling and Simulation  – Hardware-in-the-Loop (HIL):  PHIL/CHIL, and EMT 
 ●  Laboratory  Testing  –  high  power  laboratory  testing  for  isolation,  protection  strategies, 
 power quality performance 
 ●  Pilot  Projects  –  potential  benefits  include  faster  pilot  deployment/time  to  data,  lower 
 costs,  faster  validation  of  reliability,  controls,  and  communications  before  advancing  to 
 higher voltage classes.

 12.  The path forward - Key Takeaways for Stakeholders 
 The  transition  to  high-density  DC  architectures  in  AI  data  centers  is  no  longer  a  theoretical 
 pursuit  –  it  is  a  technical  necessity.  As  this  white  paper  has  outlined,  the  efficiency,  scalability, 
 and  reliability  gains  offered  by  DC  systems  are  clear.  However,  the  path  to  global  adoption  is 
 currently constricted by a standardization landscape that was built for an AC world. 
 While  AC  systems  benefited  from  a  century  of  refinement,  the  DC  ecosystem  is  being  forced  to 
 mature  in  a  fraction  of  that  time.  We  are  operating  in  a  "standardization  gap"  where  innovation  is 
 outstripping  formal  regulation.  This  has  created  a  unique  set  of  challenges,  from  the  lack  of 
 unified  guidance  on  system-level  topologies  to  the  urgent  need  for  a  validated  DC  arc  flash 
 methodology. 
 ●  The  Foundation  Exists:  Despite  gaps,  existing  product-level  standards  and  proven 
 architectures  from  the  marine,  industrial,  and  EV  sectors  can  serve  as  a  launching  pad 
 for DC design and deployment. 
 ●  Collaboration  is  Non-Negotiable  :  The  "Known  Unknowns"  of  DC  technology,  such  as 
 complex  utility  interfaces  and  high-power  protection,  cannot  be  solved  in  isolation. 
 Success  requires  active  participation  and  alignment  between  SDOs  like  UL,  IEC,  IEEE 
 and NFPA. 
 ●  Proactive  Risk  Mitigation  :  Until  formal  installation  codes  like  the  2029  NEC®  are 
 finalized,  organizations  must  lean  on  interim  guidance,  utilizing  Outlines  of  Investigation 
 (OOI),  conducting  hazard-based  risk  assessments,  and  leveraging 
 Hardware-in-the-Loop (HIL) simulations. 
 ●  Voltage  and  Power  Quality  Alignment:  Key  focus  for  further  work  to  align  the  industry 
 and  further  the  discussion  specifically  on  scaling.  Sub  workstream  focus  is  needed  for 
 the next version of the paper. 
 ●  DC  Arcflash  Rules:  Need  work  to  be  focused  on  and  aligned  as  an  industry  as  this 
 analysis  as  it  is  the  current  bottleneck  for  the  supply  chain  development  and  installation 
 for the direct current infrastructure.

 ●  Power  Distribution  -  Rack  and  Power  Alignment:  A  sub-workstream  for  the  next 
 version  of  the  paper  to  fully  understand  the  handoff  to  support  collaboration  and

 By  closing  the  gap  between  the  pace  of  innovation  and  the  pace  of  regulation,  we  can  ensure 
 that  the  next  generation  data  center  power  infrastructure  is  not  only  faster  and  more  efficient  but 
 fundamentally  safer.  The  frameworks  under  development  today  will  serve  as  the  bedrock  for  a 
 decentralized, DC-powered future. 
 13.  Conclusion 
 The  shift  towards  AI-driven  workloads  is  fundamentally  reshaping  the  data  center  power 
 landscape,  demanding  greater  efficiency  and  scalability  than  traditional  AC  architectures  can 
 easily  accommodate.  This  white  paper,  a  collaborative  effort  within  The  Open  Compute  Project, 
 establishes  a  foundational  technical  framework  for  understanding  and  evaluating  Direct  Current 
 (DC)  power  distribution  systems  in  data  center  facilities.  We  have  systematically  examined  the 
 architectural  concepts,  key  design  trade-offs  –  including  voltage  selection,  polarity,  system 
 earthing,  and  protection  schemes  –  and  the  critical  interplay  with  main  system  components.  The 
 document  purposely  presents  representative  DC  approaches  observed  across  the  industry, 
 rather  than  mandating  a  singular  solution,  to  foster  an  environment  of  open  discussion  and 
 consistent  interpretation  among  all  stakeholders,  including  operators,  designers,  manufacturers, 
 utilities,  and  AHJs.  By  aligning  with  the  OCP  tenets  of  openness,  efficiency,  impact,  scale,  and 
 sustainability,  this  work  aims  to  facilitate  informed  decision-making  as  DC  architectures  continue 
 to  evolve.  While  this  paper  provides  a  robust  initial  framework,  it  concurrently  identifies  specific 
 areas  for  further  collaborative  development  to  ensure  the  path  forward  results  in  safe,  efficient, 
 and  widely-adopted  next-generation  power  solutions.  The  ongoing  convergence  of  energy  and 
 IT  infrastructure  will  continue  to  drive  a  multi-modal  power  environment  where  both  AC  and  DC 
 models must coexist and evolve.
