Section 01
Executive Summary

This report evaluates two fundamentally different CHP prime mover technologies for the Pure DC AI Factory platform at a 5 MW electrical output scale. The Cat G3520K is a proven reciprocating natural gas genset; the Bloom Energy Server is a solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) system. Both are analyzed in full CHP configuration where waste heat drives lithium bromide (LiBr) absorption chillers to cool GPU compute infrastructure.

What is CHP — Combined Heat & Power?

A conventional power plant burns fuel to generate electricity and dumps the leftover heat into the atmosphere — wasting roughly 50–60% of the fuel's energy. CHP captures that waste heat and puts it to work. In the Pure DC architecture, hot exhaust gases and engine cooling circuits are routed through heat exchangers into absorption chillers, which use heat — instead of electricity — to produce chilled water that cools the GPU racks. The result: one unit of natural gas simultaneously generates electricity and drives the cooling system, dramatically improving overall fuel utilization. The key metric is recoverable thermal output — the more heat a prime mover produces at usable temperatures, the more "free" cooling capacity the platform gains. This is why thermal output outweighs electrical efficiency in the decision matrix.

Engineering Recommendation

For the Pure DC architecture — where waste-heat-driven absorption cooling is the central design thesis — the Cat G3520K is the recommended prime mover. It delivers 44% more recoverable thermal energy (5,000 kW vs 3,467 kW), translating to approximately 305 additional refrigeration tons of absorption chilling capacity. This thermal advantage directly offsets GPU cooling loads without consuming electrical output for mechanical chillers.

The Bloom Energy Server offers superior electrical efficiency (54% vs 45.8%) and dramatically lower maintenance burden, but its thermal output deficit undermines the core CHP-cooling integration that makes the Pure DC platform economically viable. At 3–5× the capital cost per kW, the Bloom's fuel savings of $135–272K/year cannot close the $10–21M CAPEX gap within any reasonable payback horizon.

A potential hybrid architecture — Cat for CHP base load with Bloom supplementing electrical capacity — merits further study as a Phase 2 expansion strategy.

Cost Context

Bloom's annual fuel savings of $136K–$272K are meaningful but must be weighed against the capital cost differential. At an estimated $10–21M higher CAPEX for Bloom at this scale (see Section 05), simple payback on fuel savings alone exceeds 40–150 years. The fuel efficiency advantage does not compensate for the capital premium in any realistic financial model.

Section 05
Competitive Assessment

Cat G3520K — Strengths & Weaknesses

5 Strengths
01
Thermal output dominance

5,000 kW recoverable heat drives ~995 RT of absorption chilling — 44% more than Bloom. Directly supports GPU cooling without consuming electrical output.

02
Higher exhaust grade

397°C exhaust (vs 367°C avg) provides better thermodynamic quality for absorption cycle and more margin for heat exchanger approach temperatures in Gulf Coast conditions.

03
Capital cost advantage

~$800–1,000/kW installed vs $3,000–5,000/kW for Bloom. At 5 MW, this represents a $10–15M+ capital savings — decisive for project financing.

04
Local service ecosystem

Louisiana Cat dealers provide parts, service techs, and overhaul capability within the Gulf Coast industrial corridor. Established relationships from Scott Tomsu's construction background.

05
Mechanical load following

Reciprocating engine responds to load transients in seconds. Critical for 800V DC architecture where GPU compute loads vary and power quality must be maintained.

5 Weaknesses
01
Relentless maintenance burden

Spark plugs (2–4K hrs), oil changes (500–1K hrs), top-end overhauls (30–40K hrs), major overhauls (60–80K hrs). Estimated $15–25/MWh maintenance cost. Requires dedicated staff or service contract.

02
16% higher fuel consumption

45.8% vs 54% electrical efficiency requires 11.14 MW fuel input vs 9.63 MW — an additional $136–272K/year in gas costs that compounds annually.

03
Noise, vibration, and emissions

Two V20 engines at 1500 rpm generate significant noise and vibration. NOx at 246–504 mg/Nm³ requires LDEQ air permitting and potentially catalytic aftertreatment.

04
Major overhaul downtime

Top-end and major overhauls require 2–4 weeks per unit. At continuous duty, these intervals arrive every 3–5 years. Requires N+1 planning or scheduled downtime windows.

05
No fuel flexibility path

The G3520K operates on natural gas only. No hydrogen readiness or biogas capability without significant derating. If clean fuel mandates tighten, the platform is locked in.

Bloom Energy Server — Strengths & Weaknesses

5 Strengths
01
Superior electrical efficiency

54% vs 45.8% — an 18% improvement in fuel-to-wire conversion. Every kW of electrical output goes to revenue-generating GPUs; Bloom maximizes watts per dollar of gas.

02
Near-zero maintenance

No oil, spark plugs, valve adjustments, or vibration monitoring. SOFC is solid-state electrochemistry. Maintenance ~$5–10/MWh vs $15–25/MWh for reciprocating. Fewer onsite personnel needed.

03
Clean emissions profile

Negligible NOx/SOx, no particulates, lower CO₂/MWh. Simplifies LDEQ air permitting in Lafayette and strengthens ESG narrative for investors and community engagement.

04
Modular 325 kW scaling

Deploy incrementally — start at 2 MW, add blocks as GPU cassettes come online. Cat forces 2.5 MW increments. Better matched to phased deployment strategy.

05
Hydrogen-ready fuel flexibility

SOFC platform runs on natural gas, biogas, hydrogen, or blends. As Gulf Coast hydrogen infrastructure develops (leveraging petrochemical H₂ production), this provides long-term optionality.

5 Weaknesses
01
Thermal deficit undermines CHP thesis

~690 RT vs Cat's ~995 RT — a 305 RT shortfall. Requires supplemental electric chillers consuming ~267 kW of electrical output (at COP 4), directly reducing net power available for GPUs.

02
Capital cost 3–5× higher

$3,000–5,000/kW vs $800–1,000/kW for Cat. At 5 MW: $15–25M (Bloom) vs $4–5M (Cat). Fuel savings of $136–272K/yr yield 40–100+ year simple payback — never pencils out.

03
Stack degradation cycles

SOFC stacks degrade 0.5–1.5%/year. Replacements every 5–7 years are significant cost events. Typically structured into PPA/service agreements, creating long-term contract dependency.

04
Limited load following

SOFCs don't ramp quickly — thermal cycling stresses ceramic cells. Variable GPU compute loads on the 800V DC bus require supplemental energy storage or grid tie for transients.

05
Thin Gulf Coast service infrastructure

Bloom is headquartered in San Jose with deployment density in California, Northeast, and South Korea. Gulf Coast support is sparse compared to Cat's established dealer network in Louisiana.
