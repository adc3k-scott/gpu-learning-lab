How AI Factories Work

You've heard the term. Nobody explained it. This page walks through everything — what an AI factory is, what's inside one, why power and cooling are the hardest problems, and why the technology choices we made are different from everyone else. No engineering degree required.

01
Why Old Data Centers Can't Do What We Do

For 30 years, data centers were built around CPUs — the main chip inside your laptop. A CPU is really smart. It handles complex tasks, one at a time, very fast. It's like one expert working alone.

AI doesn't work that way. Training an AI model requires millions of simple math problems happening at the same time. That's what a GPU does. A GPU has thousands of cores running in parallel. It's like having an army instead of one expert.

That switch from CPU to GPU changes everything about the building the computers live in. A CPU rack uses about 15 kilowatts. A GPU rack uses 40 to 200+ kilowatts. That's not a bigger version of the same thing — it's a completely different machine with completely different power, cooling, and networking requirements.

Old Data Centers — CPU Era
~15 kW per rack

Designed for the internet. One task at a time. Air cooled. Works fine for web pages, email, and databases. Try to run AI on it and you're fighting the building. The power wasn't designed for it. The cooling wasn't designed for it. The network wasn't designed for it.

AI Factories — GPU Era
40–200+ kW per rack

Built for AI from day one. Thousands of cores working at the same time. Liquid cooled. NVIDIA-integrated. Every piece — power, cooling, networking — designed together. No retrofits. No band-aids. Purpose-built.

02
What Is an AI Factory?

An AI factory is a building — or a set of containers — filled with powerful computers that run 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. Those computers do work that keeps the modern world running.

Every time you open your phone, check your bank account, stream a movie, use GPS, ask ChatGPT something, or pay with a card — your request travels to a server in an AI factory, gets processed in milliseconds, and comes back to you. You never see it. It's always there.

The Best Analogy

Think of it like a power plant — but for information. A power plant burns fuel and turns it into electricity that flows to every home. An AI factory takes electricity and turns it into computing power that flows to every device. Without power plants, no lights. Without AI factories, no internet, no AI, no digital economy.

05
The #1 Problem Is Heat

A single AI chip generates as much heat as a kitchen toaster. Now put 10,000 of them in a building. That's a serious problem.

Old data centers used big air conditioners — massive fans and chiller systems blasting cold air through raised floors and hot aisles. That worked when chips used 15 kW per rack. It doesn't work when chips use 200 kW per rack. Air simply can't move heat away fast enough. And even when it can, the air conditioning itself eats up 30 to 50 percent of the facility's total power budget. You're spending a third of your electricity bill just keeping the computers cool.

NVIDIA's solution: liquid cooling built into every rack. Hot water at 45°C runs directly through the rack and pulls heat straight off the chips. It's far more efficient than air. It installs in about 2 hours. And it supports racks that air cooling could never handle.

Air Cooled — Industry Average
1.4 PUE

That means for every 1 kilowatt of computing you do, you spend 0.4 kilowatts just on cooling. 29% of your electric bill goes to cooling, not computing. Max about 30 kW per rack. Can't support next-generation hardware.

NVIDIA Liquid Cooled — Our Facilities
1.02 PUE

Only 2% overhead. 98% of your electricity goes to actual computing. Supports 200+ kW per rack. Works with every generation of GPU hardware, now and in the future. The difference at scale is millions of dollars per year.

PUE stands for Power Usage Effectiveness. It's the standard measure of how efficient a facility is. Lower is better. 1.0 would be perfect — every watt goes to computing, none to overhead. The industry average is 1.3 to 1.5. We run at 1.02. Every point of difference is money that either goes to profit or gets passed to customers as lower pricing.

Our Power Strategy — Three Layers

Most AI factories plug directly into the electrical grid. When the grid gets stressed — and it will, as AI factories multiply everywhere — their costs go up and reliability goes down. We do it differently.

Layer 1 — Primary

Natural Gas + Solar + Battery

Our main power comes from on-site generation — natural gas engines and fuel cells — backed by solar panels and battery storage. Louisiana sits on top of some of the cheapest natural gas in the world (Henry Hub pricing is set 40 miles from our main site). We generate our own power at wholesale cost. We're not buying electricity at retail. We're making it.

Layer 2 — Backup

Electrical Grid

The grid is our backup, not our plan. We can pull from it if needed. But we don't depend on it. This means we're not competing with homes and businesses for electricity. We don't drive up anyone's power bill. The grid is a safety net — one we rarely need.

Layer 3 — Emergency

Diesel Generators

If the gas pipeline goes down and the grid goes down at the same time — like Hurricane Ida, when both were out for weeks — diesel keeps us running. It doesn't depend on pipelines or transmission lines. Just fuel in a tank. That's the Ida lesson: have something that depends on nothing but what's on your property.

Why This Matters to Louisiana

AI factories are coming to Louisiana whether anyone likes it or not. The question is whether they drain the grid and raise your power bill — or whether they generate their own power, use the cheapest gas in the country, and treat the grid as a last resort. We chose the second path.

Three Types of On-Site Power

When people hear "on-site generator" they picture a diesel unit — fuel tank, engine, done. That's one option, and it's the weakest one. There are two more types that work completely differently. Understanding the difference between them explains our entire power architecture.

Type 1 — Emergency Backup Only

Diesel Generator

Burns petroleum diesel. Starts in seconds. Simple to maintain. Those are the advantages — and they stop there.

Diesel is expensive per kilowatt-hour. You need a large fuel tank on-site. It creates emissions. And here's the big one: diesel generators waste all their heat. The exhaust goes straight up the stack and disappears. There's no way to capture it and use it for anything. That's called "no CHP capability" — we'll explain CHP in a moment.

Our role for diesel: Layer 3 emergency only. It keeps us running when gas and grid both fail. That's all it does.

Type 2 — CHP Primary Power

Natural Gas Engine — Cat G3520K

Same basic idea as a diesel — a big piston engine. But it burns natural gas instead of diesel. This is a 20-cylinder engine about the size of a shipping container. It makes 2.5 megawatts of electricity per unit.

Here's where it gets interesting: the engine runs hot. The jacket water (engine coolant) and the exhaust together produce an enormous amount of heat that can be captured. Route that heat through a piece of equipment called an absorption chiller and you get free cooling for your GPU racks — without spending any extra electricity on it.

That's CHP — Combined Heat and Power. One fuel input, two useful outputs: electricity and free cooling. Our role: CHP primary power for edge sites and bridge deployments.

Type 3 — Clean Primary Power

Solid Oxide Fuel Cell — Bloom Energy

This one surprises most people. A fuel cell is not an engine. There's no combustion. Natural gas goes in, electricity comes out — through a chemical process, not burning. Think of it like a battery that never runs out as long as it has gas.

Bloom Energy makes these in 325-kilowatt modules. They're quiet. Almost no moving parts. Near-zero emissions — no smoke, no NOx, nothing visible. And they're more electrically efficient than the gas engine: 54% of the fuel becomes electricity, vs 45.8% for the Cat engine.

The trade-off: less heat output than the gas engine, so less free cooling from CHP. Our role: primary DC power at main sites where Bloom modules are available.

Combined Heat & Power — Burning Fuel Once to Do Two Jobs

Here's a fact about generators that most people never think about: a standard generator only converts about half the fuel's energy into electricity. The other half leaves as heat through the exhaust pipe. It just disappears into the air. Every dollar of gas you buy — half of it is wasted.

CHP captures that wasted heat and puts it to work.

In an AI factory, cooling is one of the biggest operating expenses. GPU racks run incredibly hot. Traditional facilities use electric chillers — machines that consume a large amount of electricity to make cold water that cools the servers. So you're burning gas to make electricity, then spending that electricity on cooling. You're paying for cooling twice.

With CHP, the engine's hot exhaust and coolant heat get routed into a different kind of chiller — called an absorption chiller. An absorption chiller uses heat instead of electricity to make cold water. The exhaust that was going up the stack now cools your GPU racks instead. You paid for gas to make electricity — the cooling comes along for the ride, for free.

Without CHP — How Most Facilities Work

Pay for Cooling Twice

Burn gas → make electricity → spend that electricity running chillers → cool the racks. The exhaust heat goes up the stack and disappears. You bought that energy. You just threw it away. This is how the vast majority of AI factories operate today.

With CHP — The ADC Approach

Cooling Comes Free

Burn gas → make electricity → capture exhaust heat → run absorption chillers → cool the racks. Same fuel. Same electricity output. The cooling is now a free byproduct of power you were already generating. One dollar of gas does the work that used to cost two.

What Is an Absorption Chiller?

A regular chiller uses a compressor and electricity — like your home air conditioner, just much bigger. An absorption chiller uses heat as its energy source instead of electricity. Lithium bromide salt is the working fluid. When you pump heat into it, it produces cold water. No compressor. No electrical draw. It turns waste heat into useful cooling. For an AI factory running a CHP gas engine, this means 5,000 kilowatts of recovered engine heat can produce nearly 1,000 tons of free cooling — enough for hundreds of GPU racks.

What Does "Edge" Mean?

Most AI computing today is concentrated in a few places — Northern Virginia, Phoenix, a handful of big markets. Your data travels over a thousand miles to get processed, then travels back. That works for most things. It doesn't work for everything.

Edge AI factories put computing power closer to where the work actually happens — right near the factory floor, the oil field, the hospital, the port. When a drilling rig needs AI-powered analysis of seismic data, it doesn't want to wait for a round trip to Virginia. It needs computing nearby.

Analogy

Think of it like electricity. Power plants generate electricity in large quantities, but you need substations near your neighborhood to deliver it at usable voltage. Edge AI factories are the substations of the digital world — they bring computing power closer to where it's needed, instead of forcing everything to route through one distant hub.

The Gulf Coast is an ideal location for edge AI. Oil and gas, petrochemical plants, ports, energy companies — all of them need high-performance computing nearby. Putting AI factories in Louisiana instead of Virginia cuts latency, improves reliability, and keeps economic value in the state.

The Four Networks Inside an AI Factory

Most people think an AI factory is just a room full of computers with cables running to the internet. It's not. A real AI factory runs four completely separate networks at the same time, each doing a different job.

Network 1

The Fast Lane — Compute Network

This is how GPUs talk to each other. When you're training an AI model, thousands of GPU cores need to share data with each other constantly, at extreme speed. This network carries the heaviest load and needs to be the fastest. The technology used is called InfiniBand — purpose-built for this kind of traffic. Nothing else comes close for AI workloads.

Network 2

The Data Pipeline — Storage Network

This moves training data and model files between storage arrays and GPUs. Think of it as the supply line. If data doesn't arrive fast enough, the GPUs sit idle waiting for it. A starved GPU is a wasted GPU. This network needs high throughput — it typically runs on high-speed Ethernet or a dedicated storage fabric.

Network 3

The Control Room — In-Band Management

This is the software control layer. Monitoring, updates, job scheduling, performance data — all the "run the fleet" operations happen here. When AI orchestration software assigns a workload to a set of GPUs, this is the network it uses to give the orders. Runs on standard Ethernet.

Network 4

The Emergency Line — Out-of-Band Management

This is completely separate from everything else. It gives hardware-level access to every machine — even if the server's operating system has crashed and won't respond. Power cycling a server remotely, accessing the BIOS, controlling a machine that's completely frozen. It's like having a physical hand on every server in the building, from anywhere in the world. Kept separate for security.

Why This Matters

Every AI factory module we build has all four networks designed in from day one. That's what "purpose-built" actually means. Not just servers and power — the entire network fabric is engineered together so every piece works with every other piece, not bolted on after the fact.

InfiniBand vs Ethernet — Two Types of Connection

Ethernet is the networking technology you already know. It connects your laptop to the router. It's everywhere. Reliable, universal, and constantly getting faster. Been around since the 1980s.

InfiniBand is different. It was built specifically for supercomputers and AI. It has much higher bandwidth and much lower latency than Ethernet. When thousands of GPU cores need to stay in sync with each other — sending and receiving data every microsecond — InfiniBand is what makes that possible.

Ethernet — The Universal Standard

Good for most things

Storage access, management traffic, monitoring, internet connectivity, serving results to customers. Most of what happens in an AI factory runs on Ethernet. Fast, cheap, and proven for 40+ years. Not the right tool for GPU-to-GPU compute traffic at scale.

InfiniBand — Built for AI

The compute backbone

GPU-to-GPU communication during training and large-scale inference. When thousands of GPUs need to synchronize in microseconds, nothing else is fast enough. Non-negotiable for serious AI workloads at scale. NVIDIA's DGX SuperPOD — the gold standard reference design — uses InfiniBand for the compute fabric. So do we.

Our Approach

We use both. InfiniBand for the compute fabric — GPU to GPU. Ethernet for everything else — storage, management, client traffic. This is the same split NVIDIA uses in their own reference design, because it's the right tool for each job.

FAQ
Common Questions
Is this like a bitcoin mining operation?
No. Bitcoin mining uses specialized hardware for one single purpose — solving a specific math problem over and over. AI factories house general-purpose GPU servers — the same technology powering ChatGPT, cancer detection software, autonomous vehicles, and defense systems. The hardware is far more sophisticated. The applications create real economic value across every industry. Completely different things.
How loud is it?
Remarkably quiet. NVIDIA's integrated liquid cooling eliminates the need for loud server fans inside the racks. The outdoor dry coolers that reject heat from the facility produce sound similar to commercial HVAC — about what you'd hear standing next to a large air conditioner. Traditional air-cooled AI factories are much louder. Liquid cooling is quieter and more efficient.
How much water does it use?
Zero. The AI factory module uses dry coolers — like a car radiator — instead of evaporative cooling towers. Traditional AI factories can consume millions of gallons of water per year for cooling. Our facilities use none. The liquid in the cooling system is a closed loop — it circulates and rejects heat to the outside air through the dry cooler fins, no water evaporated.
How much power does a single module need?
A standard module is in the 3-megawatt class — roughly equivalent to 2,000 to 2,500 homes. It requires a medium-voltage power connection, either from a generator set or a utility feed. Sites can start at lower capacity and scale up to full power as additional racks come online.
Can it survive a hurricane?
The AI factory module is built inside an ISO shipping container — one of the most structurally rugged enclosures ever designed. They were built to survive ocean crossings in storms. Our 3-layer power architecture was specifically designed around the Hurricane Ida lesson: Louisiana's gas pipeline infrastructure survived Ida intact. The power grid did not. So gas is Layer 1 and diesel (which depends on nothing but on-site fuel) is Layer 3. Both layers function independently of the grid.
Who controls the data?
You do. Unlike cloud services — where your data lives on someone else's hardware in a location you can't control — an AI factory module is a privately owned asset at your location. Data never leaves unless you send it. This is critical for oil and gas, healthcare, defense, and government customers where data sovereignty isn't optional.
Does it require a building permit?
In most jurisdictions, the module is classified as equipment rather than a building. Equipment placement typically requires utility permits and site approval, but not traditional building permits — which dramatically speeds up deployment. Requirements vary by parish. We handle the permitting process for every deployment.
What is a "ton of cooling" and why does it matter?
A "ton" of cooling (technically a refrigeration ton, or RT) is a unit that measures how much heat a cooling system can remove. One RT removes about 3.5 kilowatts of heat. When we say the Cat gas engine in CHP mode provides ~995 RT of free cooling, that means it removes the equivalent of about 3,500 kilowatts of heat from the building — without spending any electricity to do it. At a 5-megawatt site, that's a major portion of the total cooling load covered by waste heat that would otherwise have gone out the exhaust stack.
What's the difference between the Bloom fuel cell and the Cat gas engine in simple terms?
Think of the Cat engine like a very big, very efficient car engine running on natural gas. It burns the gas, spins a generator, and makes electricity. But because it burns fuel, it gets hot — and that heat can be captured and used to cool the building for free. The Bloom fuel cell doesn't burn anything. It converts gas to electricity through chemistry, like a battery. It's cleaner, quieter, and more electrically efficient. But because there's no combustion, there's less waste heat to capture for cooling. Both have a place in our architecture.
