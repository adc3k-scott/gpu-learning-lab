Power Architecture

4-Layer Power Hierarchy

Four layers, strict hierarchy. Solar offsets the top, natural gas carries the backbone, diesel sits on standby for emergencies, and the grid exists only as a sell-back revenue channel.

SOLAR NATURAL GAS DIESEL GRID
Layer 1
Solar
Primary Offset
1,830 First Solar Series 7 panels across all rooftop surfaces. Approximately 1 MW capacity. Offsets daytime grid draw. Excess feeds Layer 4 sell-back. First Solar's factory is 30 miles away in New Iberia — shortest supply chain in the industry.
Layer 2
Natural Gas
Backbone 24/7
Always running. Carries the main compute load around the clock. Henry Hub pricing — Louisiana is at the source. Caterpillar reciprocating engines provide reliable, dispatchable power that solar cannot. This is the layer that guarantees uptime.
Layer 3
Diesel Gensets
Emergency Only
On-site fuel. Pipeline-independent. Cummins American-made generators. Only activates if gas supply is interrupted. Fuel stored on-site in the infrastructure yard. Zero dependency on external pipelines during emergencies.
Layer 4
Grid (LUS)
Sell-Back Only
NOT a power source. Excess generation goes back to the LUS grid for revenue. The grid connection exists to sell power, not to consume it. Trappeys generates its own — the grid is a customer, not a supplier.
Why this order matters: Most facilities put grid first and hope for the best. Trappeys puts generation first and treats the grid as a revenue stream. Solar and gas cover 100% of load. Diesel covers 100% of emergencies. The grid buys our excess.

Thermal Management

Water Tower Cooling

The iconic Trappeys water tower becomes thermal infrastructure. Heritage meets function.

Liquid-Cooled Racks to Thermal Mass

NVIDIA ships every NVL72 rack with an integrated liquid cooling loop running at 45 degrees C. Hot water exits the rack and enters the facility's Coolant Distribution Unit (CDU). The CDU transfers heat to the external rejection system — either dry coolers or the water tower's thermal mass.

The Trappeys water tower provides significant thermal buffer capacity. During peak compute loads, the tower absorbs excess heat. During low-load periods, ambient cooling brings the tower back to baseline. This passive thermal mass smooths temperature spikes without additional energy input.

The result: lower cooling energy costs, a repurposed landmark, and a cooling system that leverages physics instead of fighting it.
