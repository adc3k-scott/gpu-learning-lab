Est. 1898 • Lafayette, Louisiana

From Hot Sauce to AI Factory

Bernard Francis Trappey started bottling pepper sauce at his blacksmith shop on Avery Island in 1898. By 1918, B.F. Trappey's Sons, Inc. was running a bottling plant in New Iberia. In 1929, the family purchased an 8-acre tract on the Vermilion River in Lafayette — the former Baldwin Lumber Company and Star Salt Company — and opened canning operations in 1930.

During World War II, this facility produced over 12 million pounds of canned candied yams for U.S. and Allied military forces. What started as a family pepper sauce operation became a global food company with more than 100 international brokerage representatives.

At peak operations in the 1960s, Trappeys employed 350-600 workers seasonally and produced over 10 million cans of sweet potatoes annually, distributed worldwide. The plant also canned okra (Randolph Trappey Sr. patented an okra-trimming machine) and continued the original pepper sauce line.

The cannery closed in 1992 after nearly a century of continuous family operation. The nine contributing structures — including the iconic water tower — have been determined eligible for the National Register of Historic Places (period of significance: 1930-1972). The site sat vacant for over 30 years. Now it gets a second life.

Why Trappeys

On March 20, 2026, we tried to rent a single GPU on RunPod — the largest cloud GPU marketplace on the planet. Every GPU type, every region, every cloud tier: sold out. Not just the high-end H100s. RTX 3080s. V100s from 2017. Six-gigabyte cards. All of it. Gone.

That's not a supply blip. That's structural demand exceeding global infrastructure. Every researcher, every startup, every student competing for the same scarce hardware — and losing.

Trappeys changes that for Louisiana. Local compute. Local power. Local sovereignty. No queue, no stockout, no depending on the rest of the world to leave something on the shelf. A historic cannery reborn as the state's first NVIDIA-certified AI factory — half a mile from UL Lafayette, powered by the cheapest natural gas in the country, offset by solar panels made 30 miles away.

This isn't speculation. The demand is here. The site is here. The technology is ready. We just need Lafayette to say yes.
